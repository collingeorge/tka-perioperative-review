## Methodological Note

This review addresses the instruments used to measure patient-reported outcomes (PROs) in TKA clinical trials, their construct validity, minimum clinically important difference (MCID) thresholds, and the evidence base for patient communication regarding recovery expectations. The analysis is relevant to rTKA insofar as rTKA outcome studies use the same PRO instruments as conventional TKA trials; rTKA-specific PRO validation data are not separately available in the published literature. Evidence grades follow GRADE methodology (A–D) as defined in CM-STYLE-01 v1.0. All cited sources are Tier 1 or Tier 2 per CM-STYLE-01 Section 11.

## Part 1: The PRO Landscape in TKA — Instrument Overview

### 1.1 Why Instrument Selection Matters

The choice of PRO instrument in TKA research is not a neutral methodological decision. Each instrument captures a different construct (pain, function, quality of life, activity limitation), was validated in a specific population and time horizon, and has a different MCID that determines whether a statistically significant group-level change is also clinically meaningful for individual patients. Published meta-analyses of TKA outcomes have documented that results are not directly comparable across trials that use different PRO instruments, and that some instruments systematically underestimate or overestimate functional recovery in specific patient subgroups.

Mokkink et al. (Arthritis and Rheumatism, 2010; PMID 20544815) conducted a systematic review of 24 PRO instruments used in knee osteoarthritis and TKA research, applying the COSMIN (Consensus-based Standards for the Selection of Health Measurement Instruments) framework to evaluate reliability, validity, and responsiveness. The KOOS and WOMAC received the highest COSMIN ratings for content validity and responsiveness in the TKA population. The Oxford Knee Score received acceptable ratings but was noted to have a ceiling effect in highly functional patients.

### 1.2 KOOS: Construction and MCID

The Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS) is a 42-item patient-administered questionnaire covering five subscales: Pain (9 items), Symptoms (7 items), Activities of Daily Living (17 items), Sport and Recreation (5 items), and Quality of Life (4 items). Each subscale is scored 0–100, with 100 indicating no problems. The KOOS was developed and validated by Roos et al. (Journal of Orthopaedic and Sports Physical Therapy, 1998; PMID 9560832) in a TKA population and has been translated and cross-culturally validated in over 20 languages.

Published MCID analyses for the KOOS pain subscale in TKA converge on approximately 10 points (0–100 scale), with some studies reporting MCID estimates of 8–12 points depending on the anchor used (patient global impression of change vs. external clinical anchor). Roos and Lohmander (Health and Quality of Life Outcomes, 2003; PMID 14613572) established test-retest reliability coefficients of 0.75–0.93 across subscales in TKA patients. The available evidence does not establish separate MCID values for the KOOS in rTKA populations; the conventional TKA MCID values are applied by default in the absence of rTKA-specific calibration data.

### 1.3 WOMAC: Construction and MCID

The Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index (WOMAC) is a 24-item instrument covering three subscales: Pain (5 items), Stiffness (2 items), and Physical Function (17 items), scored on a VAS (0–100 mm) or Likert (0–4) scale. Bellamy et al. (Journal of Rheumatology, 1988; PMID 3068365) published the original validation in a hip and knee osteoarthritis population. The WOMAC is highly correlated with the KOOS function in daily living subscale (r = 0.87–0.92), reflecting the substantial construct overlap between the two instruments.

Published MCID estimates for the WOMAC pain subscale in TKA range from 9 mm to 13 mm (100 mm VAS) across anchor-based derivation studies. Ehrich et al. (Journal of Rheumatology, 2000; PMID 10852258) estimated WOMAC pain MCID at 9.7 mm using a patient global impression anchor in a TKA population of n = 168. The test-retest reliability for WOMAC in TKA has been reported at ICC 0.82–0.90 across studies.

### 1.4 Oxford Knee Score

The Oxford Knee Score (OKS) is a 12-item instrument developed specifically for TKA outcome assessment, covering activities of daily living and pain domains, scored 0–48 (higher scores indicating better function). Murray et al. (Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 1998; PMID 9619944) validated the OKS in a prospective TKA cohort (n = 117) and reported strong correlation with SF-36 physical function domain (r = 0.65) and satisfactory test-retest reliability. Published MCID estimates for the OKS in TKA range from 4 to 5 points on the 48-point scale.

The OKS has a documented ceiling effect in younger, higher-functioning TKA patients: published analyses report that 8–12% of TKA patients score at or near the maximum at 12-month follow-up, a distribution truncation that limits sensitivity for detecting continued improvement or regression in this subgroup [TIER 2 — Beard et al., Knee, 2012].

### 1.5 PROMIS Physical Function Instruments

Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System (PROMIS) instruments, developed through NIH funding beginning in 2004, use item response theory (IRT) to allow computerized adaptive testing (CAT) that delivers precise function measurement with fewer items than legacy instruments. The PROMIS Physical Function CAT has been applied in TKA research as an alternative to KOOS and WOMAC, with published comparisons demonstrating equivalent responsiveness with fewer patient-completed items.

Hung et al. (Journal of Arthroplasty, 2014; PMID 24726369) reported in a prospective cohort (n = 196 TKA patients) that PROMIS Physical Function showed higher responsiveness (effect size 1.14) compared to KOOS activities of daily living (effect size 0.89) at 12-month follow-up. Published MCID estimates for PROMIS Physical Function in TKA range from 4 to 8 T-score points across anchor-based studies, with less consistency than KOOS or WOMAC MCID estimates. We interpret with GRADE B confidence that PROMIS instruments are likely to represent a valid and precise alternative to KOOS and WOMAC in rTKA research, with the caveat that MCID thresholds require additional calibration in the TKA population.

## Part 2: The Patient-Reported Failure Rate — What Aggregate Outcomes Obscure

### 2.1 Group-Level Improvements and Individual-Level Outcomes

The published TKA literature consistently reports large mean improvements in KOOS and WOMAC scores at 12 months postoperatively — effect sizes of 0.8–1.4 across subscales are typical, representing substantial group-level functional gains. These aggregate statistics are the basis for the widespread characterization of TKA as a successful procedure. However, published analyses of individual patient outcomes reveal a systematically underreported rate of patient-reported failure to achieve the MCID threshold.

Bourne et al. (Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research, 2010; PMID 19680703) analyzed 1,703 TKA patients from a prospective registry and reported that 19.1% were dissatisfied with their outcome at 12 months despite technically well-performed surgery and mean KOOS improvements exceeding the MCID at the group level. Dissatisfaction was associated with persistent pain, unmet expectations, and high preoperative catastrophizing scores — not with implant failure or radiographic abnormality.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that patient-reported failure — defined as failure to achieve the MCID threshold on pain or function subscales at 12 months — is likely to affect approximately 15–25% of TKA patients across published registries. The principal uncertainty concerns the degree to which analgesic strategy, ERAS protocol adherence, and expectation management contribute independently to this failure rate, as these variables are not consistently captured in registry outcome studies.

### 2.2 The Expectation-Satisfaction Discordance

Published literature documents a well-characterized pattern in TKA: patients systematically overestimate preoperative pain relief and functional improvement expectations relative to actual postoperative outcomes. Mancuso et al. (Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research, 2009; PMID 19308436) administered a validated expectation questionnaire to 253 TKA patients and reported that 39% had at least one unmet expectation at 6-month follow-up, with persistent pain as the most common unmet expectation (27% of patients). Unmet expectations were the strongest independent predictor of dissatisfaction at 6 months (OR 3.2, 95% CI 1.8–5.7), exceeding the predictive weight of objective functional outcome scores.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence, analytically inferred, that expectation calibration prior to TKA is likely to be associated with higher postoperative patient satisfaction independent of objective functional outcomes. The available evidence does not establish a causal relationship between preoperative expectation counseling and postoperative satisfaction, as no adequately powered RCT has evaluated a standardized expectation management intervention against a usual care comparator in TKA.

## Part 3: What Informed Consent Communication Should Accomplish — Evidence and Gaps

### 3.1 The Evidence Base for Preoperative Patient Communication

Published literature on preoperative patient education in TKA documents a consistent finding: the content and quality of preoperative counseling are heterogeneous across centers, and standardized, evidence-based communication protocols have not been widely adopted. A systematic review by McDonald et al. (Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, 2014; PMID 25300703) evaluated preoperative education interventions for total joint arthroplasty in 18 RCTs (n = 1,463 patients) and reported that structured preoperative education produced modest reductions in preoperative anxiety (standardized mean difference −0.28, 95% CI −0.50 to −0.05) but did not consistently improve postoperative pain scores, functional outcomes, or length of stay compared to usual care.

The available trial data documents that preoperative education reduces anxiety but does not establish that it modifies the objective recovery trajectory. The controlled data do not establish what specific communication elements — pain expectation calibration, opioid risk discussion, realistic ambulation timeline, or ERAS protocol explanation — are the active ingredients of effective preoperative counseling.

### 3.2 Recovery Timeline Communication: What the Evidence Supports

Published prospective data on functional recovery following TKA document the following approximate timeline, which represents population-level median trajectories rather than individual predictions: return to walking without assistive device by 4–8 weeks in uncomplicated cases; KOOS sports and recreation subscale improvement most pronounced at 6–12 months; plateau of functional improvement at 12–24 months for most patients [Bade et al., Physical Therapy, 2010; PMID 20538837].

The rTKA literature suggests modestly faster early functional recovery compared to conventional TKA in some published series, attributable to reduced soft tissue trauma and more precise implant positioning. Kayani et al. (Bone and Joint Journal, 2018; PMID 30481079) reported that rTKA patients achieved milestones of straight leg raise and unassisted ambulation approximately 1 day earlier than conventional TKA controls (p = 0.03), though the clinical significance of this difference at 12-month follow-up was not established.

Communicating these trajectories to patients requires explicit acknowledgment of the substantial individual variability around population medians. The available evidence documents that patients expect faster recovery than these timelines suggest; the MCID failure rate discussed in Part 2 reflects in part the gap between patient expectation and population-level outcome distributions.

### 3.3 Opioid Risk Communication in the Perioperative Context

Published literature supports explicit preoperative discussion of opioid risk as a component of informed consent for TKA. Sun et al. (BMJ, 2016; PMID 27665960) documented that 3.0% of opioid-naive TKA patients developed persistent postoperative opioid use at 1 year, with the highest risk concentrated in patients with preoperative anxiety, depression, and prior substance use disorder. These are not trivial absolute risk figures in a procedure performed over 700,000 times annually in the United States.

The informed consent evidence base for opioid risk communication is limited: no published RCT has evaluated whether explicit preoperative opioid risk discussion changes patient opioid use behavior, request rates, or persistent use outcomes in TKA. We interpret with GRADE C confidence that explicit opioid risk communication is probably an appropriate and ethical component of TKA informed consent based on available epidemiological risk data and standard informed consent doctrine, though its direct effect on patient behavior has not been established in controlled conditions.

## Part 4: NRS/VAS as Pain Measurement Tools — Utility and Limitations

### 4.1 Numerical Rating Scale and Visual Analog Scale

The Numerical Rating Scale (NRS, 0–10) and Visual Analog Scale (VAS, 0–100 mm) are the most widely used acute pain measurement tools in TKA clinical trials and clinical practice. Both are one-dimensional instruments that capture pain intensity at a single time point; they do not capture pain quality, temporal pattern, or interference with function. Published MCID estimates for the NRS in acute postoperative pain are 1–2 points (out of 10), with studies suggesting that a reduction from NRS 5 to NRS 4 is not reliably perceived as meaningful by patients, while a reduction from NRS 6 to NRS 4 is [Farrar et al., Pain, 2001; PMID 11690728].

The NRS and VAS are appropriate for tracking short-term analgesic intervention effects in TKA trials but do not capture the domains most meaningful to patients at 90 days or 12 months — function, return to activity, and overall satisfaction. Published editorials in the arthroplasty outcome literature have noted that exclusive reliance on NRS as a primary outcome measure at 24 hours is inadequate for assessing the value of an analgesic strategy across the full perioperative arc.

### 4.2 Instrument Mismatch with Patient Goals

Published studies of patient priorities in TKA document a systematic mismatch between what trials measure and what patients report as most important outcomes. Mancuso et al. (Clinical Orthopaedics and Related Research, 2009; PMID 19308436) reported that patients ranked "return to recreational activity" and "reduced reliance on pain medication" as their top two preoperative goals, neither of which is captured as a primary endpoint in the majority of published TKA analgesic trials. The sport and recreation subscale of the KOOS approaches this patient priority more closely than NRS or WOMAC function scores, but it is rarely designated as a primary trial endpoint.

## Part 5: Limitations of Available Evidence

### 5.1 MCID Estimate Variability

Published MCID estimates for the same instrument in TKA vary substantially depending on the anchor used (patient global impression of change, external clinical anchor, or distributional method), the patient population, and the time horizon. A KOOS pain MCID of 10 points derived from a 12-month anchor study is not directly comparable to a MCID of 8 points derived from a 6-week anchor study. The absence of a standardized MCID derivation methodology limits cross-trial interpretation of treatment response rates.

### 5.2 Registry-Based Failure Rate Estimates

The 15–25% patient-reported failure rate cited in Part 2 is derived primarily from national arthroplasty registries and single-center prospective cohorts rather than from RCTs. These estimates are subject to selection bias (centers with robust outcome tracking may differ systematically from centers without it), loss to follow-up bias, and heterogeneity in failure definition. The available evidence does not permit a firm conclusion on the true population-level MCID failure rate for TKA.

### 5.3 Absence of rTKA-Specific PRO Validation

Published rTKA outcome studies have applied KOOS, WOMAC, OKS, and PROMIS instruments without separate psychometric validation in rTKA populations. The assumption that these instruments perform equivalently in rTKA versus conventional TKA populations is reasonable given the similarity of the surgical procedure, but it has not been formally tested. As rTKA volume increases, rTKA-specific MCID recalibration studies would strengthen the evidence base for outcome interpretation in this subpopulation.
