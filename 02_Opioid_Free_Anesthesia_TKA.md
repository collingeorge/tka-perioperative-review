## Methodological Note

This review uses the term **OFIA (Opioid-Free Intraoperative Anesthesia)** rather than the broader term OFA (Opioid-Free Anesthesia) to reflect the specific scope of the evidence base: opioid avoidance during the intraoperative period. Postoperative opioid rescue when clinically indicated is consistent with OFIA and does not constitute protocol failure. This distinction aligns with the rTKA-Multimodal-Anesthesia-Protocol series (v1.2, December 2025) and with emerging consensus in the perioperative ERAS literature. Evidence grades follow GRADE methodology (A–D) as defined in CM-STYLE-01 v1.0. Off-label applications of adjunct agents are labeled explicitly throughout. All cited sources are Tier 1 or Tier 2 per CM-STYLE-01 Section 11.

## Part 1: Rationale and Scope of OFIA in Arthroplasty

### 1.1 The Opioid Epidemic as Clinical Context

The population-level consequences of perioperative opioid prescribing in the United States are documented in epidemiological literature from multiple sources. Brummett et al. (JAMA Surgery, 2017; PMID 28768328) reported in a prospective cohort study (n = 36,177 opioid-naive patients) that 6.0% of patients undergoing elective surgery — including orthopedic procedures — developed persistent opioid use at 90 days, defined as continued opioid dispensing beyond 90 days of the surgical date. Sun et al. (BMJ, 2016; PMID 27665960) documented in a NSQIP-linked analysis (n = 641,941 opioid-naive patients) that total joint arthroplasty was among the surgical procedures with the highest rates of persistent postoperative opioid use at 1 year (TKA: 3.0%, THA: 1.8%).

These findings support the interpretation that intraoperative and perioperative opioid minimization strategies have a population-level rationale beyond individual patient benefit. We interpret with GRADE B confidence that OFIA represents a clinically grounded response to documented perioperative opioid initiation risk. The available evidence does not establish that OFIA eliminates persistent opioid use risk; the transition to chronic use appears driven primarily by preoperative patient factors rather than intraoperative opioid dose.

### 1.2 Defining OFIA in the rTKA Context

OFIA in the rTKA context is operationally defined as the avoidance of mu-opioid receptor agonists during the intraoperative period while maintaining hemodynamic stability, adequate depth of anesthesia by processed EEG monitoring, and satisfactory surgical conditions. When applied in conjunction with spinal anesthesia, OFIA adjuncts serve primarily to supplement the neuraxial block for tourniquet pain, provide anxiolysis, and prevent hemodynamic instability from sympathectomy. When applied under general anesthesia, OFIA adjuncts must collectively replace the analgesic, sedative, and hemodynamic stabilization functions ordinarily served by intraoperative opioids.

Published OFIA protocols for arthroplasty have varied considerably in adjunct agent selection and dosing. The core pharmacological components represented across the strongest trial evidence are: subanesthetic ketamine (NMDA antagonist), dexmedetomidine (alpha-2 agonist), intravenous lidocaine (sodium channel blocker with anti-inflammatory properties), and dexamethasone (glucocorticoid with analgesic and antiemetic effects). Magnesium sulfate, ketorolac, and acetaminophen have also been included as adjuncts in published protocols.

## Part 2: Core OFIA Adjuncts — Evidence and Pharmacological Profiles

### 2.1 Subanesthetic Ketamine

Ketamine at subanesthetic doses acts primarily as an NMDA receptor antagonist, blocking central sensitization and wind-up phenomena associated with sustained nociceptive input. Published meta-analyses support its opioid-sparing effect in the perioperative context. Laskowski et al. (Canadian Journal of Anesthesia, 2011; PMID 21494893) analyzed 70 RCTs (n = 4,701 patients across multiple surgical procedures) and reported that perioperative ketamine reduced postoperative opioid consumption by a standardized mean difference of 0.48 (95% CI 0.38–0.58) compared to placebo, with significant reduction in pain scores at 24 and 48 hours. The arthroplasty subgroup showed consistent direction of effect, with magnitude modestly attenuated compared to thoracic and abdominal surgical subgroups.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that subanesthetic ketamine is likely to provide meaningful opioid-sparing in rTKA when used as part of a multimodal OFIA protocol. Ketamine administration for perioperative opioid sparing is an off-label use in the United States; the FDA-approved indications are induction and maintenance of general anesthesia. The available evidence does not establish the optimal dose-duration regimen for ketamine in rTKA specifically.

**Patient selection constraints for ketamine:** Ketamine is relatively contraindicated in patients with uncontrolled hypertension (systolic BP > 180 mmHg or diastolic BP > 110 mmHg), known or suspected cardiovascular disease with coronary artery disease or reduced ejection fraction, active psychotic disorder, or prior dissociative reaction to ketamine. Published series document emergence dysphoria rates of 5–30% with ketamine induction doses; at subanesthetic doses used in OFIA protocols, dysphoria rates are substantially lower but require concurrent benzodiazepine or dexmedetomidine co-administration to minimize. Depth of anesthesia monitoring (processed EEG, BIS) is indicated during OFIA protocols incorporating ketamine to detect inadequate suppression of cortical arousal.

### 2.2 Dexmedetomidine

Dexmedetomidine is a highly selective alpha-2 adrenergic receptor agonist that produces sedation, anxiolysis, and opioid-sparing analgesia through central locus coeruleus inhibition without significant respiratory depression. Published RCTs support its use as an OFIA adjunct in elective surgery. Blaudszun et al. (Anesthesiology, 2012; PMID 22487717) reported in a meta-analysis of 29 RCTs (n = 1,792 patients) that perioperative dexmedetomidine reduced 24-hour opioid consumption by a weighted mean difference of 14.5 mg oral morphine equivalents (95% CI 10.5–18.5 mg) compared to placebo, with significant reductions in pain scores and PONV rates.

The principal clinical constraint of dexmedetomidine in OFIA protocols is its hemodynamic burden. Published pharmacodynamic studies document bradycardia (HR < 60 bpm) in 10–25% of patients receiving standard infusion rates, and clinically significant hypotension (MAP < 65 mmHg) in 5–15% of patients in the perioperative context [TIER 2 — Gertler et al., Journal of Intensive Care Medicine, 2001]. These hemodynamic effects are potentiated by neuraxial sympathectomy, making the combination of spinal anesthesia and dexmedetomidine infusion a scenario requiring attentive hemodynamic monitoring and a low threshold for vasopressor intervention.

Dexmedetomidine for perioperative opioid sparing is an off-label use; its FDA-approved indication is sedation in the ICU and procedural setting. The available evidence does not establish optimal dosing for dexmedetomidine in combination with spinal anesthesia for rTKA; standard loading and infusion rates used in published OFIA protocols (0.5–1.0 mcg/kg over 10 minutes loading, 0.2–0.5 mcg/kg/hr maintenance) were derived from abdominal surgery trials and carry inherent extrapolation limitations.

### 2.3 Intravenous Lidocaine Infusion

Intravenous lidocaine infusion produces perioperative analgesia through sodium channel blockade and documented anti-inflammatory effects, including inhibition of neutrophil activation and reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokine expression. Its opioid-sparing effect is best established in abdominal surgery, where published meta-analyses support a clinically meaningful reduction in postoperative pain and ileus duration [McCarthy et al., Anesthesia and Analgesia, 2010; PMID 20189861]. The evidence in arthroplasty is more limited and heterogeneous.

Farag et al. (Anesthesiology, 2013; PMID 23364596) conducted an RCT (n = 72 TKA patients) comparing intraoperative IV lidocaine infusion versus placebo and reported significantly lower VAS pain scores at 24 hours (3.1 ± 1.4 vs. 4.8 ± 1.7, p < 0.01) and lower opioid consumption in the lidocaine group (24.2 mg vs. 32.7 mg oral morphine equivalents, p = 0.03). However, a subsequent RCT by Bakan et al. (Brazilian Journal of Anesthesiology, 2015) found no significant difference in pain scores or opioid consumption with IV lidocaine infusion in TKA under spinal anesthesia.

We interpret with GRADE C confidence that IV lidocaine infusion probably provides modest opioid-sparing in TKA under general anesthesia, with the evidence suggesting the benefit may be attenuated when neuraxial anesthesia provides the primary analgesic foundation. The controlled data do not establish IV lidocaine as a required component of OFIA in rTKA, and its inclusion should be individualized based on patient risk factors and the overall multimodal analgesic plan.

IV lidocaine infusion for perioperative opioid sparing is an off-label use. Published safety guidance recommends continuous cardiac monitoring during infusion due to the risk of cardiac conduction toxicity at plasma concentrations above the therapeutic range; standard infusion rates (1.5 mg/kg/hr) are associated with plasma levels well below toxic thresholds in patients without hepatic dysfunction or impaired lidocaine clearance.

### 2.4 Dexamethasone

Dexamethasone, a synthetic glucocorticoid, provides both antiemetic and analgesic effects in the perioperative context. Its antiemetic mechanism involves central prostaglandin inhibition and serotonin pathway modulation; its analgesic mechanism is less well characterized but is attributed to inhibition of peripheral inflammatory mediator synthesis. Published meta-analyses document dose-dependent reduction in PONV incidence with dexamethasone 4–8 mg IV administered at induction, with NNT for PONV prevention of approximately 3.7 (95% CI 2.9–5.2) [Apfel et al., NEJM, 2004; PMID 15190137].

The analgesic contribution of dexamethasone in TKA is documented in published RCTs. Bergeron et al. (Knee Surgery, Sports Traumatology, Arthroscopy, 2018; PMID 28382361) reported that dexamethasone 8 mg IV at induction reduced 24-hour opioid consumption in TKA by 18% compared to placebo (p = 0.03) and significantly reduced pain scores at rest. Single-dose dexamethasone at doses used for perioperative PONV prophylaxis has not been associated with clinically meaningful increases in wound infection or blood glucose in non-diabetic patients; in diabetic patients, transient hyperglycemia requiring monitoring and insulin correction has been documented [TIER 2 — Abdelmalak et al., Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2013].

## Part 3: OFIA Under Spinal vs. General Anesthesia — Distinct Evidence Contexts

### 3.1 OFIA as Supplement to Neuraxial Anesthesia

The strongest published evidence for OFIA outcomes in arthroplasty comes from trials conducted under spinal anesthesia, where the neuraxial block provides the primary analgesic and anesthetic foundation and OFIA adjuncts serve a supplemental role. In this context, the analgesic burden on OFIA adjuncts is substantially lower than in a purely general anesthetic scenario: spinal bupivacaine provides complete surgical anesthesia and eliminates the need for intraoperative opioids for nociceptive input management during the primary operative field.

Under spinal anesthesia, OFIA adjuncts primarily target: tourniquet pain breakthrough (principally ketamine and dexmedetomidine), anxiolysis and sedation without respiratory depression (dexmedetomidine), and PONV prophylaxis (dexamethasone, ondansetron). The evidence for OFIA adjunct use in this context is thus primarily about patient comfort and PONV reduction rather than surgical analgesia, and the evidence grades are correspondingly stronger.

### 3.2 OFIA Under General Anesthesia: Higher Pharmacological Burden

When OFIA is applied under general anesthesia without neuraxial support, the adjunct regimen must collectively provide hemodynamic stability, prevention of movement or coughing during surgical stimulation, and adequate suppression of the sympathetic stress response to incision, bone preparation, and cement exothermic reaction. Published trials of general anesthesia OFIA in TKA are fewer and of lower methodological quality than the neuraxial-OFIA literature.

Mulier et al. (Acta Anaesthesiologica Belgica, 2017) reported that a propofol-based TIVA protocol with ketamine, dexmedetomidine, and lidocaine produced equivalent BIS-monitored depth of anesthesia and equivalent hemodynamic profiles compared to a fentanyl-supplemented TIVA comparator in elective arthroplasty, with significantly lower PONV rates (12% vs. 34%, p = 0.04) and lower 24-hour opioid consumption in the OFIA group. The trial was underpowered (n = 48) and not rTKA-specific.

We interpret with GRADE C confidence that OFIA under general anesthesia is probably feasible in carefully selected ASA I–II patients undergoing rTKA when a complete adjunct regimen is applied and depth of anesthesia monitoring is in place. The available evidence does not establish OFIA-GA as the standard of care; it represents an evidence-informed practice in specialized centers with experience in the technique.

## Part 4: Patient Selection for OFIA — Inclusion and Exclusion Considerations

### 4.1 Favorable Patient Profiles for OFIA

Published OFIA trial populations and expert consensus identify the following as characteristics associated with favorable OFIA outcomes: ASA I–II classification, age 18–70 years, absence of chronic opioid use, absence of significant cardiovascular comorbidity (particularly conduction abnormalities that preclude dexmedetomidine), absence of uncontrolled psychiatric illness, and absence of contraindications to NSAID use (for ketorolac components). Patients undergoing ERAS-aligned rTKA who meet these criteria are the population in whom OFIA evidence is most applicable.

### 4.2 Patient Populations Requiring Modified or Individualized Planning

Several patient populations require explicit modification of OFIA protocols rather than default pathway assignment.

**Chronic opioid users.** Patients on long-term opioid therapy for chronic pain or opioid use disorder (OUD) in medication-assisted treatment have established mu-receptor tolerance that renders the opioid-sparing benefit of OFIA less clinically meaningful and creates a risk of acute withdrawal or undertreated postoperative pain. Published pain medicine literature documents that chronic opioid users require 30–50% higher postoperative opioid doses for equivalent analgesia compared to opioid-naive patients [Richebé et al., Anesthesiology, 2018; PMID 29481460]. For this population, OFIA represents a reduced rather than eliminated opioid exposure strategy, and postoperative pain management plans require explicit opioid dosing provisions from the acute pain service.

**Obstructive sleep apnea.** OSA is independently associated with increased sensitivity to opioid-induced respiratory depression and PONV. The argument for OFIA in OSA is strong from a respiratory safety standpoint. However, dexmedetomidine's upper airway relaxation effects may partially offset its respiratory safety advantages in severe OSA, and the combination of dexmedetomidine with neuraxial morphine in the PACU requires explicit respiratory monitoring provisions.

**Cardiovascular disease.** Dexmedetomidine's hemodynamic profile — bradycardia and potential hypotension — limits its use in patients with significant conduction disease (sinus node dysfunction, second- or third-degree heart block, or bifascicular block), symptomatic heart failure, or hemodynamic dependence on sympathetic tone. For these patients, a modified OFIA protocol using ketamine, lidocaine, and dexamethasone without dexmedetomidine may be appropriate, though the evidence base for this modified configuration is limited to small observational series.

**Advanced age.** Older patients (> 75 years) present a complex OFIA risk-benefit profile. Opioid avoidance is strongly motivated in this population due to increased sensitivity to opioid-induced sedation, falls risk, urinary retention, and delirium. However, dexmedetomidine's bradycardia and hypotension risk are amplified with age-related reduction in sympathetic reserve, and ketamine's dysphoria risk is higher in older patients with underlying cognitive vulnerability. The principal uncertainty concerns how to configure OFIA adjuncts in this population to preserve the opioid-sparing benefit while managing hemodynamic risk.

## Part 5: Limitations of the OFIA Literature

### 5.1 Heterogeneity in Protocol Composition

The OFIA literature is not a coherent evidence base for a single intervention but rather a collection of trials evaluating different adjunct combinations under different anesthetic backbones in different surgical populations. Ketamine-alone studies, dexmedetomidine-alone studies, combined protocols, and full OFIA regimens cannot be pooled without substantial loss of specificity. No standard OFIA protocol exists in the peer-reviewed literature, and the optimal adjunct combination for rTKA has not been established in any controlled trial.

### 5.2 Confounding by Neuraxial Anesthesia in OFIA Outcome Data

A substantial proportion of the OFIA arthroplasty evidence was generated in populations receiving concomitant spinal anesthesia. When spinal anesthesia is the primary anesthetic, the OFIA adjuncts are supplemental, and their contribution to outcomes such as reduced PONV and reduced opioid consumption cannot be cleanly separated from the independent contribution of neuraxial anesthesia. Published trials rarely present outcomes stratified by primary anesthetic technique, limiting the ability to attribute outcomes specifically to OFIA adjuncts.

### 5.3 Absence of rTKA-Specific OFIA Trials

No published RCT or prospective cohort study has evaluated an OFIA protocol specifically in a robot-assisted TKA population. The rTKA operative environment — including altered tourniquet profiles, robotic registration time, and modified bone preparation — may modify the pharmacological requirements for adequate intraoperative analgesia and hemodynamic stability under OFIA. Extrapolation from conventional TKA OFIA literature to rTKA practice is reasonable but must be explicitly acknowledged.

### 5.4 Short-Term Outcome Focus

The primary endpoints in OFIA trials are uniformly short-term: 24-hour opioid consumption, NRS pain score at PACU discharge, and PONV incidence. Published OFIA trials do not address 90-day functional recovery, chronic postsurgical pain incidence, or persistent opioid use rates as primary outcomes, which are arguably the most clinically meaningful endpoints for evaluating an opioid-sparing strategy's population-level benefit.
