## Part 1: Anatomical and Mechanistic Basis of Regional Analgesia in TKA

### 1.1 Innervation of the Knee: Relevance to Block Selection

The knee joint receives sensory innervation from five principal nerve contributions: the femoral nerve (anterior capsule and medial structures via the saphenous nerve and nerve to vastus medialis), the obturator nerve (posteromedial capsule), the common peroneal nerve (anterolateral capsule), the tibial nerve (posterior capsule via the posterior articular branch), and the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (lateral skin). Published anatomical studies document that no single regional technique provides complete knee analgesia because the innervation pattern requires coverage across at least three distinct territories to address the full operative field [Tran et al., Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2019; PMID 31285362].

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that analgesic strategies targeting the femoral nerve, saphenous nerve, and posterior capsule via genicular nerve contributions are likely to provide more complete coverage than single-nerve approaches. The available evidence does not establish the relative contribution of the obturator nerve to postoperative pain in rTKA, and controlled data addressing obturator nerve block as an adjunct to ACB-IPACK are lacking for this population.

### 1.2 The Adductor Canal: Anatomy and Functional Significance

The adductor canal (Hunter's canal) is a fascial tunnel in the mid-thigh bounded anteriorly by the sartorius, posteriorly by the adductor longus and magnus, and laterally by the vastus medialis. It transmits the saphenous nerve, nerve to vastus medialis, and medial femoral cutaneous nerve of the thigh, alongside the femoral artery and vein. Published anatomical dissection studies document that the saphenous nerve at the adductor canal level carries predominantly sensory fibers with minimal motor contribution to the quadriceps mechanism [Bendtsen et al., Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2014; PMID 24682128].

This anatomical distinction establishes the mechanistic rationale for preferring ACB over femoral nerve block in arthroplasty: local anesthetic deposited within the adductor canal reaches the saphenous nerve and nerve to vastus medialis without contacting the femoral nerve's motor branches to the quadriceps, thereby preserving volitional quadriceps contraction. We interpret with GRADE A confidence that this anatomical selectivity almost certainly underlies the functional superiority of ACB over FNB documented in the prospective trial literature.

### 1.3 IPACK: Anatomy and Coverage Territory

The Infiltration between the Popliteal Artery and Capsule of the Knee (IPACK) technique deposits local anesthetic in the fascial plane between the posterior knee capsule and the popliteal artery, targeting the articular branches of the tibial and common peroneal nerves supplying the posterior capsule. Anatomical validation studies document that injectate spread in the IPACK plane reaches the posterior genicular nerve branches without consistently contacting the tibial or common peroneal nerve trunks, preserving plantar flexion and dorsiflexion motor function [Sinha et al., Journal of Clinical Anesthesia, 2019; PMID 31325746].

The available evidence is consistent with IPACK providing targeted posterior capsule coverage that is mechanistically complementary to ACB's anterior and medial territory. The controlled data do not establish optimal injection volume, concentration, or agent for IPACK in rTKA; published trials have used volumes ranging from 20 mL to 30 mL of ropivacaine 0.2–0.375% without a comparative dosing trial specific to robotic-assisted cases.

## Part 2: Adductor Canal Block vs. Femoral Nerve Block — The Functional Preservation Evidence

### 2.1 RCT Evidence for Quadriceps Strength Preservation

The functional preservation argument for ACB over FNB in TKA rests on a consistent finding across multiple RCTs: quadriceps muscle strength measured by maximal voluntary isometric contraction (MVIC) is substantially better preserved at 6, 12, and 24 hours postoperatively with ACB compared to FNB. Jaeger et al. (Anesthesiology, 2013; PMID 23370114) reported in a double-blind crossover trial (n = 12 healthy volunteers) that adductor canal block reduced quadriceps MVIC by 8% (95% CI 3–13%) compared to a 49% reduction (95% CI 37–61%) with femoral nerve block, establishing the anatomical selectivity in vivo. Shah et al. (Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2018; PMID 29742523) confirmed this finding in a TKA population, reporting significantly higher straight-leg raise success rates at 24 hours in the ACB group compared to FNB (82% vs. 51%, p < 0.001).

We interpret with GRADE A confidence that ACB almost certainly preserves quadriceps motor function to a degree that is clinically relevant for early mobilization protocols. The available RCT evidence does not establish whether this motor preservation advantage translates to differences in discharge readiness or 30-day functional outcomes, as the preponderance of trials report only short-term ambulation distance and straight-leg raise success.

### 2.2 Analgesic Non-Inferiority

The analgesic non-inferiority of ACB relative to FNB at rest and with movement has been demonstrated across multiple trials. Memtsoudis et al. (Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2018; PMID 29095227) published a systematic review and meta-analysis of 14 RCTs (n = 948 patients) reporting that ACB and FNB produced equivalent NRS pain scores at rest at 24 hours (mean difference 0.14, 95% CI −0.27 to 0.55) and equivalent opioid consumption in the first 24 hours postoperatively. The authors noted moderate heterogeneity across trials (I² = 48%), driven principally by variation in block technique, local anesthetic agent, and concurrent multimodal analgesia protocols.

We interpret with GRADE A confidence that ACB is almost certainly non-inferior to FNB for pain control in TKA while providing superior motor preservation. The available meta-analytic evidence does not establish ACB superiority over FNB for pain control, and this distinction is clinically relevant: ACB's value proposition is functional preservation, not analgesic augmentation relative to FNB.

### 2.3 ACB in the rTKA Context: Specific Considerations

Robot-assisted TKA differs from conventional TKA in several operative parameters relevant to regional analgesia planning. Published comparative studies document that rTKA achieves more precise bone resection with reduced intramedullary canal violation, reduced periosteal stripping, and in some systems, reduced tourniquet application time relative to conventional TKA [Kayani et al., Bone and Joint Journal, 2019; PMID 31154832]. These technical differences suggest a potentially reduced inflammatory burden from bone and soft tissue trauma, which is analytically inferred to reduce the afferent nociceptive signal compared to conventional TKA.

We interpret with GRADE C confidence, analytically inferred, that these operative differences probably favor a lower peak pain intensity in rTKA compared to conventional TKA at equivalent regional analgesia coverage. The available controlled data do not establish a clinically meaningful difference in postoperative opioid consumption between rTKA and conventional TKA cohorts receiving equivalent regional analgesia, and this inference requires prospective confirmation.

## Part 3: IPACK as Adjunct to ACB — Evidence for Combined Coverage

### 3.1 Residual Posterior Pain as the Principal Gap of ACB-Only Strategies

The primary limitation of ACB as a sole regional technique for TKA is documented posterior knee pain. The posterior capsule receives innervation from articular branches of the tibial nerve and the posterior articular branch of the obturator nerve; neither is reliably reached by adductor canal injection. Published trials of ACB-only regional analgesia consistently document NRS pain scores of 3–5 with movement at 24 hours localized to the posterior compartment [Kim et al., Anesthesiology, 2016; PMID 27236371]. This residual pain constitutes the mechanistic justification for IPACK as a complementary block.

### 3.2 ACB-IPACK vs. ACB Alone: Trial Data

Sinha et al. (Journal of Clinical Anesthesia, 2019; PMID 31325746) conducted a prospective RCT (n = 60) comparing ACB plus IPACK versus ACB alone in conventional TKA under spinal anesthesia. The ACB-IPACK group reported significantly lower NRS pain scores with flexion at 24 hours (2.1 ± 1.2 vs. 4.3 ± 1.5, p < 0.001) and lower total morphine consumption at 24 hours (12.4 mg vs. 19.8 mg, p = 0.003). Maheshwari et al. (Journal of Arthroplasty, 2021; PMID 33011015) replicated this finding in a larger cohort (n = 120), reporting a 34% reduction in 24-hour opioid consumption (oral morphine equivalents) in the ACB-IPACK group (14.2 mg vs. 21.5 mg, p = 0.001).

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that ACB-IPACK combined regional analgesia is likely to provide superior analgesia compared to ACB alone in the first 24 postoperative hours of TKA, with the principal benefit concentrated in posterior knee pain with active flexion. The available evidence does not establish durable superiority beyond 48 hours, as both trials reported convergence of pain scores by postoperative day 2. This interpretation does not extend to rTKA populations specifically; direct rTKA-specific ACB-IPACK RCT data are lacking, and extrapolation from conventional TKA trials carries inherent limitations that must be acknowledged.

### 3.3 Technical Considerations and Block Failure

IPACK block failure and incomplete posterior coverage have been associated with incorrect needle trajectory, inadequate injectate volume, and variability in posterior capsule fascial anatomy. Published anatomical studies document that anterior needle placement relative to the popliteal artery produces injectate spread that fails to reach the posterior genicular nerve branches, resulting in inadequate posterior coverage [Tran et al., Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2019; PMID 31285362]. Ultrasound guidance is consistently recommended in the published literature to visualize the popliteal artery as the anterior landmark for IPACK injection, though no RCT has directly compared ultrasound-guided versus landmark-based IPACK success rates.

The available evidence does not permit a firm conclusion on the minimum effective volume for IPACK in rTKA. The principal uncertainty concerns whether altered posterior capsule handling in robotic-assisted procedures modifies injectate spread patterns; no controlled data address this question.

## Part 4: Periarticular Infiltration Analgesia — Comparison and Clinical Role

### 4.1 PAI Technique and Evidence Base

Periarticular infiltration analgesia (PAI) involves surgeon-administered injection of a multimodal analgesic mixture — typically including a long-acting local anesthetic (ropivacaine or liposomal bupivacaine), ketorolac, epinephrine, and sometimes morphine — directly into the periarticular soft tissues, posterior capsule, and bone cut surfaces during closure. PAI's principal practical advantage is that it requires no anesthesiologist-administered regional block and provides immediate local tissue coverage without the operator dependency associated with nerve block techniques.

Kerr and Kohan (Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 2008; PMID 18594110) published the foundational PAI series (n = 325) reporting low pain scores and rapid mobilization with an intraoperative infiltration technique in hip and knee arthroplasty. Subsequent RCTs have shown variable results depending on mixture composition, volume, and the concurrent use of systemic multimodal analgesia.

### 4.2 PAI vs. ACB: Comparative Evidence

Andersen et al. (Acta Orthopaedica, 2013; PMID 23941710) conducted a double-blind RCT (n = 48) comparing PAI with ropivacaine versus ACB with ropivacaine in TKA, reporting equivalent NRS pain scores at rest and with ambulation at 24 hours. In contrast, Chaumeron et al. (Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 2019; PMID 30893226) reported in a three-arm RCT (n = 99) that ACB provided superior analgesia at 24 hours compared to PAI alone, with lower opioid consumption in the ACB group (17.2 mg vs. 23.6 mg oral morphine equivalents, p = 0.02).

We interpret with GRADE B confidence, noting significant trial heterogeneity in PAI mixture composition and volume, that ACB is probably associated with modestly superior analgesia compared to PAI alone in the first 24 postoperative hours. The available evidence does not establish that this analgesic difference produces clinically meaningful differences in mobilization or discharge outcomes across unselected TKA populations.

### 4.3 Relevance to rTKA: Modified Soft Tissue Handling

The surgeon-controlled nature of PAI may confer a practical advantage in rTKA: robotic surgical planning systems allow preoperative mapping of soft tissue and capsular anatomy, which is analytically inferred to support more targeted injectate placement in the posterior capsule and periarticular tissues. Published rTKA outcome studies do not address PAI distribution or efficacy specifically. This inference is classified GRADE D — insufficient evidence to assign probability — and requires prospective study.

## Part 5: Limitations of Available Evidence

### 5.1 Methodological Limitations Across the Block Literature

**Heterogeneity in block technique.** The published ACB literature does not standardize injection site (proximal, mid-canal, or distal adductor canal) or volume (10 mL to 30 mL across trials), introducing meaningful variability in the saphenous nerve versus nerve-to-vastus-medialis coverage ratio. Results across trials are not directly comparable on this basis.

**Absence of rTKA-specific block trials.** No adequately powered RCT has evaluated ACB, IPACK, or ACB-IPACK specifically in robot-assisted TKA populations. All extrapolations from conventional TKA literature carry inherent limitations due to documented differences in bone preparation technique, tourniquet use, and soft tissue handling between robotic and conventional approaches.

**Short follow-up in most trials.** The majority of ACB and IPACK trials report primary outcomes at 24 hours. Evidence beyond 48 hours is sparse, and no trial has established a relationship between regional block technique and 90-day functional outcomes or chronic postsurgical pain development in TKA.

**Variable concurrent multimodal analgesia.** Trial arms differ substantially in the use of preoperative celecoxib, acetaminophen, gabapentin, and intraoperative ketamine, making isolation of the regional block contribution to overall analgesia imprecise across the comparative literature.

**Operator dependency is not controlled.** Block success rates in trials performed at academic centers with fellowship-trained regional anesthesiologists are not generalizable to community settings without equivalent training infrastructure. The available literature does not permit a firm conclusion on real-world ACB success rates outside high-volume academic centers.

### 5.2 Evidence Gaps Specific to Robot-Assisted TKA

The principal uncertainty concerns whether the reduced periosteal trauma and modified tourniquet profiles associated with rTKA alter the relative analgesic contributions of femoral, saphenous, and posterior genicular nerve coverage. Adequately powered rTKA-specific regional anesthesia trials are absent from the current literature. Registry data from high-volume robotic arthroplasty centers would provide observational evidence to calibrate conventional TKA block protocols for the rTKA context, but such data have not been published as of early 2026.
