## Abstract

**Background:** Perioperative analgesic planning in total knee arthroplasty (TKA) spans five sequential clinical decision domains — anesthesia modality, regional analgesic technique, adjuvant pharmacology, patient risk phenotype, and outcome measurement — that have been studied largely in isolation. No prior review has mapped the scope of evidence across all five domains simultaneously or systematically examined the implications of that evidence for robot-assisted TKA (rTKA), a platform for which direct perioperative analgesic evidence is absent.

**Objective:** To map and synthesize the scope, nature, and quality of evidence for perioperative analgesic strategies across five decision domains in adults undergoing primary TKA, and to characterize evidence gaps relevant to robot-assisted TKA.

**Eligibility criteria:** English-language systematic reviews, meta-analyses, RCTs, prospective cohort studies, and major guideline documents addressing perioperative analgesic strategy in adults undergoing primary TKA. Case reports, editorials, letters, and conference abstracts without full data were excluded.

**Information sources:** PubMed/MEDLINE searched through March 21, 2026, supplemented by targeted retrieval of guideline documents from ERAS Society, AAHKS/AAOS/ASRA, ASA, and ASA Task Force publications, and backward reference tracking from foundational systematic reviews and meta-analyses.

**Selection and charting:** Single-reviewer title/abstract screening and full-text assessment. Data were charted by decision domain, study design, population, intervention, comparator, and outcome. Evidence quality was assessed using a GRADE-based interpretive overlay.

**Results:** Sixty sources were included across five decision domains. The strongest evidence (GRADE A) supports adductor canal block over femoral nerve block for quadriceps-sparing functional preservation and NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors as the highest-evidence adjuvant class. Moderate evidence (GRADE B) supports neuraxial preference for PONV reduction and early opioid exposure attenuation, the IPACK block as a complement to ACB for posterior capsular analgesia, and ketamine for opioid reduction in opioid-tolerant patients. Low evidence (GRADE C) characterizes neuraxial advantage on thromboembolic and major morbidity endpoints, and IV lidocaine and magnesium as TKA adjuvants. Risk stratification evidence demonstrates strong predictive validity for preoperative opioid use and catastrophizing but weak evidence that pathway modifications improve outcomes in high-risk subgroups. Standard perioperative outcome instruments are inadequately calibrated for high-risk phenotypes. No direct evidence addresses any of these domains in rTKA populations.

**Conclusion:** The perioperative analgesic literature in TKA contains substantial but fragmented evidence across five clinical decision domains. Risk stratification and outcome measurement are the most evidence-deficient domains relative to their clinical importance. The rTKA evidence gap is total. A risk-stratified, measurement-aware perioperative framework is warranted and should be evaluated prospectively in rTKA-specific controlled trials.

**Keywords:** total knee arthroplasty; scoping review; perioperative analgesia; adductor canal block; IPACK; opioid-free anesthesia; pain catastrophizing; patient-reported outcomes; MCID; robot-assisted arthroplasty; PRISMA-ScR

## 1. Introduction

Total knee arthroplasty is among the most frequently performed elective surgical procedures in the United States, with volumes projected to exceed four million cases annually by 2040. Clinical outcomes at the population level are well-established: TKA reliably reduces joint pain and improves function in appropriately selected patients with end-stage knee osteoarthritis. Yet patient-reported dissatisfaction after TKA persists in 15–25% of recipients, and chronic postoperative pain — defined as pain persisting beyond three months at the operative site — affects up to 20% of TKA patients across prospective cohort data. These rates have not improved consistently despite decades of surgical and anesthetic refinement.

The perioperative analgesic literature in TKA has addressed this problem in isolated fragments. Anesthesia modality trials compare spinal to general anesthesia on PONV, blood loss, and 30-day morbidity. Regional analgesia trials compare ACB to FNB on NRS scores and TUG performance. Adjuvant pharmacology trials compare opioid-free anesthesia protocols to standard care on 24-hour MME consumption. Risk stratification studies identify psychological and pharmacological predictors of poor outcomes. Outcomes measurement researchers evaluate instrument responsiveness and MCID calibration. No prior review has systematically mapped the scope of evidence across all five domains simultaneously or characterized the evidence gaps relevant to robot-assisted TKA, a platform that now represents a significant and growing proportion of TKA volume in many centers.

This scoping review maps evidence across five perioperative analgesic decision domains in adults undergoing primary TKA, synthesizes that evidence by domain, characterizes evidence quality and gaps, and reports evidence gap implications for robot-assisted TKA where direct evidence is absent. The rTKA application layer is explicitly designated as evidence-gap analysis and inference rather than established evidence.

## 2. Methods

This scoping review was conducted in accordance with JBI scoping review methodology and is reported according to the PRISMA extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR). A PRISMA-ScR checklist is provided as Supplementary File 1.

### 2.1 Review Design

This is a scoping review following JBI guidance for scoping reviews. The purpose is to map and synthesize the nature and scope of evidence for perioperative analgesic strategies across five clinical decision domains in TKA, not to conduct a pooled meta-analysis or provide a systematic assessment of all available studies on any single intervention. In accordance with JBI scoping review methodology, formal risk-of-bias scoring was not performed because the objective was evidence mapping and structured synthesis across decision domains rather than pooled effect estimation. A GRADE-based interpretive overlay was applied to the mapped evidence to characterize relative evidence quality across included source types; this overlay is supplementary to the scoping framework, not a replacement for it.

### 2.2 Review Question

Using the Population-Concept-Context (PCC) framework:

- **Population:** Adults (age ≥ 18 years) undergoing primary elective total knee arthroplasty for knee osteoarthritis.
- **Concept:** Perioperative analgesic strategies across five decision domains: (1) anesthesia modality (spinal versus general anesthesia), (2) regional analgesic technique (adductor canal block, IPACK, periarticular infiltration analgesia, femoral nerve block), (3) intraoperative adjuvant pharmacology (ketamine, dexmedetomidine, IV lidocaine, magnesium, NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors, dexamethasone, IV acetaminophen), (4) patient risk stratification (preoperative opioid use, pain catastrophizing, central sensitization, depression, anxiety, sleep disturbance), and (5) outcome measurement (KOOS, WOMAC, PROMIS, NRS, TUG, MCID calibration, PGIC).
- **Context:** Perioperative and postoperative analgesic management in primary TKA performed in hospital or ambulatory surgery settings, including evidence gap analysis for robot-assisted TKA where direct evidence is limited or absent.

The overarching review question is: *What is the scope, nature, and quality of evidence for perioperative analgesic strategies across the five identified clinical decision domains in adults undergoing primary TKA, and what are the evidence gaps and portability implications for robot-assisted TKA?*

### 2.3 Information Sources

PubMed/MEDLINE was searched as the primary bibliographic database. Searches were conducted through March 21, 2026, with no lower date limit applied. Searches were restricted to English-language publications. In addition to database searching, targeted retrieval was conducted for guideline documents and consensus statements from the following sources: ERAS Society, American Association of Hip and Knee Surgeons (AAHKS), American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS), American Society of Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine (ASRA), American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA), and ASA Task Force publications. Backward reference tracking from identified systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and guideline-grade documents was performed to capture foundational studies not identified in initial database searches. No additional databases (Embase, Cochrane Library, Scopus, Web of Science) were searched; this is acknowledged as a limitation (see Section 9).

### 2.4 Search Strategy

Searches were conducted in PubMed/MEDLINE using the following structured queries, executed through March 21, 2026. Search terms were iteratively refined across domains.

**Domain 1 — Anesthesia modality:**
`("total knee arthroplasty"[MeSH] OR "total knee replacement"[tiab]) AND ("spinal anesthesia"[MeSH] OR "general anesthesia"[MeSH] OR "neuraxial"[tiab]) AND ("postoperative complications"[MeSH] OR "pain, postoperative"[MeSH] OR "nausea and vomiting, postoperative"[MeSH])`

**Domain 2 — Regional analgesia:**
`("total knee arthroplasty"[MeSH] OR "total knee replacement"[tiab]) AND ("adductor canal block"[tiab] OR "femoral nerve block"[MeSH] OR "nerve block"[MeSH] OR "IPACK"[tiab] OR "periarticular infiltration"[tiab])`

**Domain 3 — Adjuvant pharmacology:**
`("total knee arthroplasty"[MeSH] OR "arthroplasty, replacement, knee"[MeSH]) AND ("ketamine"[MeSH] OR "dexmedetomidine"[MeSH] OR "lidocaine"[MeSH] OR "magnesium"[MeSH] OR "anti-inflammatory agents, non-steroidal"[MeSH] OR "dexamethasone"[MeSH] OR "acetaminophen"[MeSH]) AND ("pain management"[MeSH] OR "analgesics, opioid"[MeSH])`

**Domain 4 — Risk stratification:**
`("total knee arthroplasty"[MeSH] OR "total knee replacement"[tiab]) AND ("catastrophization"[MeSH] OR "pain catastrophizing"[tiab] OR "central sensitization"[tiab] OR "opioid"[tiab] OR "depression"[MeSH] OR "anxiety"[MeSH] OR "sleep"[MeSH]) AND ("treatment outcome"[MeSH] OR "prognosis"[MeSH])`

**Domain 5 — Outcome measurement:**
`("total knee arthroplasty"[MeSH] OR "total knee replacement"[tiab]) AND ("patient reported outcome measures"[MeSH] OR "KOOS"[tiab] OR "WOMAC"[tiab] OR "PROMIS"[tiab] OR "minimal clinically important difference"[tiab] OR "MCID"[tiab])`

Guideline document retrieval used organizational website searches and PubMed queries with organization names as author fields or publication source terms.

### 2.5 Eligibility Criteria

**Inclusion criteria:**
- Study design: systematic reviews, meta-analyses, randomized controlled trials, prospective cohort studies, and guideline-grade consensus documents
- Population: adults (age ≥ 18 years) undergoing primary elective TKA
- Language: English only
- Date: no lower limit; searches conducted through March 21, 2026
- Scope: perioperative analgesic strategy in at least one of the five defined domains
- Indexing: all included studies are indexed in PubMed with available PMIDs

**Exclusion criteria:**
- Case reports, case series (fewer than 20 patients), editorials, letters, and opinion pieces
- Conference abstracts without full-text published data
- Studies addressing revision TKA, unicompartmental arthroplasty, or total hip arthroplasty as the primary population unless reporting TKA subgroup data analyzed separately
- Studies addressing pediatric populations
- Non-English language publications

### 2.6 Selection Process

Title and abstract screening was performed by a single reviewer (CBG) against the eligibility criteria. Full-text assessment was performed for all sources passing title/abstract screening. Single-reviewer screening is a methodological limitation of this review and is reported transparently in the Limitations section (9.5). No duplicate independent screening was performed; no conflict resolution process was therefore required. The PRISMA flow diagram is presented as Supplementary File 2.

### 2.7 Data Charting

A standardized data charting form was applied to each included source. The following variables were extracted:

- Study design (RCT, systematic review/meta-analysis, prospective cohort, guideline document)
- Population (TKA-specific or mixed arthroplasty with TKA subgroup)
- Intervention and comparator
- Primary outcome domain (pain, functional recovery, opioid consumption, adverse events)
- Risk phenotype variables addressed (if applicable)
- Outcome measurement instruments used
- Relevance to robot-assisted TKA portability
- Evidence tier (Tier 1: RCTs, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, guideline-grade documents; Tier 2: observational, contextual, or corroborating evidence)

### 2.8 Synthesis

Included sources were synthesized by perioperative decision domain. Within each domain, evidence was organized by study design hierarchy, with systematic reviews and meta-analyses as the primary synthesis source and individual RCTs cited where foundational evidence has not yet been superseded by higher-level synthesis. A GRADE-based interpretive overlay was applied as a supplementary characterization of evidence quality within each domain; GRADE grades are interpretive designations applied to mapped evidence and are not the primary organizational method of the review. Evidence gaps and portability implications for robot-assisted TKA are reported separately for each domain under a dedicated subsection; all rTKA-specific conclusions are designated EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED to clearly distinguish inference from established evidence.

## 3. Results: Anesthesia Modality — Spinal Versus General Anesthesia

### 3.1 Evidence Scope

Included sources for this domain: 10 studies and guideline documents (Table 1). Study designs: 1 RCT (REGAIN trial, hip fracture population), 2 systematic reviews/meta-analyses (Johnson et al. 2016; Zywiel et al. 2014), 1 Cochrane review (Hu et al. 2009), 2 large observational database studies (Memtsoudis et al. 2013; Stundner et al. 2012), 2 guideline documents (Wainwright/ERAS Society 2020; Horlocker/ASRA 2018), and 2 clinical practice standards (Gonvers et al. 2021; ASA neuraxial opioids 2016). The absence of an adequately powered RCT comparing spinal to general anesthesia specifically in elective TKA with anesthesia modality as the primary endpoint is the dominant evidence gap for this domain.

### 3.2 Principal Findings

The ERAS Society guidelines³ recommend neuraxial anesthesia as the preferred modality where not contraindicated. The evidence underlying this recommendation stratifies into three tiers of quality.

The mechanistically favoured tier — PONV reduction and early opioid exposure attenuation — carries GRADE B support. The proposed mechanism (elimination of volatile anesthetic and intraoperative opioid as Apfel risk factors⁴) is robust and platform-independent. The best available comparative synthesis (Johnson et al.⁵) found no statistically significant difference in pooled PONV incidence, indicating that the mechanism-based advantage is attenuated in antiemetic-treated contemporary pathways. Intrathecal morphine (ITM) reduces 24-hour opioid consumption (Gonvers et al.⁶, GRADE B) but imposes a monitoring burden⁷ that conflicts with short-stay ERAS protocols.

The qualified tier — blood loss and transfusion reduction — carries GRADE B evidence that is era-dependent and probably attenuated in contemporary TXA-standardized practice⁹.

The weak-evidence tier — thromboembolic risk, major morbidity, mortality, and POCD — does not carry controlled evidence of the quality required to support confident claims. Observational data (Memtsoudis et al.¹⁰) are directionally consistent but structurally confounded; the REGAIN trial¹¹ found no significant major outcome differences by modality under controlled conditions.

### 3.3 Key Evidence Gaps

No RCT has evaluated spinal versus general anesthesia with anesthesia modality as the primary endpoint in elective conventional TKA or rTKA. Effect sizes for blood loss advantage are pre-TXA era data not representative of contemporary practice. The ITM monitoring-versus-discharge-readiness tradeoff in short-stay arthroplasty has not been evaluated in a controlled trial.

### 3.4 Evidence Gap — Implications for Robot-Assisted TKA

No evidence addresses anesthesia modality in rTKA. The following implications are EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED. PONV reduction and ITM opioid-sparing are mechanism-based and platform-independent; these advantages are likely preserved in rTKA. Blood loss reduction may be preserved in tourniquet-free rTKA through sympathectomy-mediated hemostasis, but the absolute clinical magnitude is probably further attenuated by rTKA's independently lower blood loss profile. ITM monitoring requirements create a practical tension with short-stay rTKA discharge protocols that has not been evaluated.

## 4. Results: Regional Analgesia — ACB, IPACK, and Periarticular Infiltration

### 4.1 Evidence Scope

Included sources for this domain: 11 studies (Table 2). Study designs: 2 foundational RCTs (Jæger et al. 2013; Shah and Jain 2014), 2 Cochrane-level meta-analyses (Zhao ACB vs FNB 2017; Fowler FNB 2008), 3 meta-analyses on IPACK (Hussain et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2023; Albrecht et al. 2021), 1 meta-analysis on PAI (Jiang et al. 2013), 1 comparative study of ACB versus PAI (Zhao et al. 2019), 1 meta-analysis on perineural dexamethasone (Albrecht et al. 2014), and 1 rTKA outcomes study for context (Xu et al. 2022; domestic robotic system, platform verification required).

| Xu et al. 2022⁵⁹ | RCT (rTKA) | TKA | Functional outcomes | Context only | rTKA associated with early functional outcomes ≥ conventional TKA |

### 4.2 Principal Findings

ACB provides non-inferior analgesia to FNB for anterior compartment pain at 24–48 hours with a significant quadriceps-sparing advantage (GRADE A, consistent across multiple RCTs and a Cochrane-level meta-analysis¹⁷). This evidence base supports ACB as the preferred peripheral nerve strategy over FNB when early ambulation is a pathway priority. The evidence does not establish ACB superiority on 90-day functional outcomes, KOOS or WOMAC at 6 weeks, or fall incidence.

IPACK addition to ACB reduces posterior knee pain and oral MME consumption at 24–48 hours in TKA patients receiving multimodal analgesia including LIA/PAI (GRADE B, consistent across three independent meta-analyses²⁰˒²¹˒²²). A critical qualification applies: the primary meta-analytic evidence for IPACK was generated in LIA co-administration contexts; additive benefit in populations not receiving PAI/LIA is not directly established.

PAI is an evidence-supported analgesic strategy with GRADE B–C evidence for opioid reduction and non-inferiority versus ACB on anterior pain; PAI and ACB-plus-IPACK address partially distinct anatomical targets and are potentially complementary.

Perineural dexamethasone extends peripheral nerve block duration by 6–8 hours (GRADE B); ACB-specific data are limited relative to the broader peripheral nerve block literature.

### 4.3 Key Evidence Gaps

All RCT evidence is derived from conventional TKA populations. No adequately powered study has evaluated ACB or IPACK efficacy in a population specifically enrolled as rTKA. Optimal IPACK technique parameters (volume, concentration, agent, adjuvant) are not standardized. The IPACK evidence base is concentrated in single-center trials with a predominantly positive published record, raising publication bias risk.

### 4.4 Evidence Gap — Implications for Robot-Assisted TKA

No direct evidence addresses ACB, IPACK, or PAI efficacy in rTKA. The following are EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED. Anatomical targets of these techniques are not substantially modified by robotic versus manual surgical approach; technique portability is intact. Three hypothesis-level implications follow from the evidence gap: (1) tourniquet-free rTKA may reduce ischemic pain demand, producing smaller absolute NRS differences between block and control than in conventional TKA trials; (2) rTKA soft tissue precision may reduce PAI and IPACK analgesic demand by reducing posterior capsular trauma; (3) rTKA ERAS pathways targeting sub-8-hour ambulation may amplify the clinical consequence of ACB's quadriceps-preservation advantage relative to the 24-hour ambulation window where most ACB versus FNB trials measured outcomes.

## 5. Results: Adjuvant Pharmacology and Opioid-Minimizing Pathways

### 5.1 Evidence Scope

Included sources for this domain: 16 studies and guideline documents (Table 3). Study designs: 3 guideline-grade systematic reviews (Hannon et al. 2023 ketamine; Fillingham et al. 2020 NSAIDs; Waldron et al. 2013 dexamethasone), 6 systematic reviews and meta-analyses across agents, 2 TJA-specific meta-analyses (Xu et al. acetaminophen), 1 RCT (PRECISION cardiovascular safety), and 4 mixed-surgical-population systematic reviews.

**Table 3. Evidence map — Adjuvant pharmacology domain**

| Agent | Primary source(s) | Design | GRADE | Key finding |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NSAIDs/COX-2 | Fillingham et al. 2020³¹ | Guideline SR (AAHKS/AAOS/ASRA) | A | "Strong evidence" for opioid-sparing and pain reduction; PRECISION³² establishes COX-2 cardiovascular safety |
| Ketamine | Hannon et al. 2023²⁴; Schwenk et al. 2021²⁶ | Guideline SR; clinical review | B | Reduces 24-hr MME; strongest effect in opioid-tolerant patients; ceiling effect in opioid-naive neuraxial pathway |

| Dexmedetomidine | Yang et al. 2020²⁷; Blaudszun et al. 2012²⁸ | Meta-analyses (TKA/THA; mixed surgical) | B | Opioid-sparing in TKA/THA; bradycardia in TKA subgroup limits use |

| Magnesium | Albrecht et al. 2013³⁰ | Meta-analysis | C | Statistically significant but clinically modest effect; no arthroplasty-specific controlled evidence |

### 5.2 Principal Findings

The evidence gradient across adjuvant agents is pronounced. NSAIDs and COX-2 inhibitors carry the strongest evidence (GRADE A) supported by a guideline-grade systematic review and an adequately powered cardiovascular safety trial. Ketamine (GRADE B), dexamethasone (GRADE B), dexmedetomidine (GRADE B), and IV acetaminophen (GRADE B) are each supported by arthroplasty-specific meta-analytic evidence. IV lidocaine and magnesium (both GRADE C) lack TKA-specific controlled evidence against a contemporary neuraxial-plus-block comparator.

A critical interpretive issue applies across this domain: the ceiling effect problem. The benefit of any individual adjuvant agent is highest when background analgesic coverage is lowest, and is attenuated when neuraxial anesthesia and regional block techniques are already providing near-complete coverage of the relevant pain generators. Effect sizes from OFA trials conducted without regional block coverage are not directly portable to contemporary neuraxial-plus-ACB pathways. This interpretive constraint is not consistently acknowledged in the primary OFA trial literature and constitutes a systematic bias in the available evidence base for this domain.

A second interpretive issue applies to bundle-level OFA evidence: when OFA experimental arms include neuraxial anesthesia, ACB, NSAIDs, dexamethasone, and ketamine while comparator arms include fewer components, observed bundle-level differences cannot be attributed to the opioid elimination strategy per se. Agent-specific evidence is the appropriate analytic unit.

### 5.3 Key Evidence Gaps

No adequately powered TKA-specific RCT has evaluated IV lidocaine against a contemporary neuraxial-plus-regional-block comparator. Magnesium sulfate lacks arthroplasty-specific controlled evidence beyond historical single-center data. Ketamine's ceiling effect in opioid-naive patients on contemporary neuraxial-plus-block pathways has not been formally evaluated in a stratified RCT. Bundle-level OFA evidence does not permit agent-level attribution.

### 5.4 Evidence Gap — Implications for Robot-Assisted TKA

Pharmacological mechanisms are platform-independent. Evidence grades assigned above port to rTKA without modification. Agents whose effect sizes are primarily driven by rescue of inadequate analgesia in high-pain operative contexts may show further attenuation in rTKA if rTKA's baseline pain burden is independently lower; this prediction applies most directly to IV lidocaine (which lacks even conventional TKA evidence) and to agents with ceiling effects in neuraxial-plus-block pathways.

### 6.2 Principal Findings

The mapped evidence establishes five preoperative risk domains with independent predictive validity for worse TKA analgesic outcomes: preoperative opioid use (GRADE A, dose-response established), pain catastrophizing (GRADE B, most consistent predictor across systematic reviews), central sensitization (GRADE B, CPM efficiency as preoperative marker), depression and anxiety (GRADE B, multiple independent prospective cohorts), and sleep disturbance (GRADE B, prospective cohort evidence).

The risk prediction evidence is substantially stronger than the evidence that specific pathway modifications improve outcomes in identified high-risk subgroups. No controlled trial has enrolled patients stratified by composite risk tier and separately evaluated pathway modification efficacy. The pathway modification framework presented in Section 6.6 is explicitly designated EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED and represents the most clinically coherent application of available risk prediction data, not an evidence-derived protocol.

The prevalence implications are clinically significant: preoperative opioid use affects approximately 20–30% of TKA candidates; elevated PCS scores (≥30) affect 15–25% of candidates in most prospective cohorts. The composite high-risk phenotype (two or more major risk domains) likely represents 20–40% of the TKA candidate population. Standard-pathway analgesic evidence derived from average-risk populations does not apply uniformly to this subgroup.

### 6.3 Key Evidence Gaps

No controlled trial has evaluated composite risk-stratified pathway modification in primary TKA. The mapping from individual risk predictors to specific analgesic pathway modification is analytically supported but empirically untested. Risk-stratified subgroup analyses are absent from most published perioperative analgesic RCTs in this domain.

### 6.4 Risk-Stratified Pathway Modification Framework — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED

The following three-tier framework is designated EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED and PROTOCOL-CONDITIONED. It is a hypothesis-generating structure for future controlled evaluation, not an evidence-derived clinical protocol.

**Standard-risk** (opioid-naive, PCS <20, no active psychological comorbidity, normal sleep, no widespread pain): standard analgesic pathway based on best available evidence per Sections 3–5. Population-average effect sizes apply directly.

**Elevated-risk** (one major risk domain — opioid use <90 MME/day, or PCS 20–29, or single active psychological comorbidity): pathway modification indicated. Ketamine should be added to the adjuvant base for its central sensitization and opioid tolerance benefits. Preoperative psychological evaluation is appropriate. Outcome expectations should be calibrated to risk-stratified rather than population-average data.

**High-risk** (two or more major risk domains, or fibromyalgia phenotype, or opioid use >90 MME/day, or PCS ≥30): pathway modification required. Multimodal adjuvant pharmacology maximized within contraindication limits; ketamine is the highest-priority agent. Preoperative psychological intervention — CBT for catastrophizing, sleep treatment, depression management — should be completed where possible before elective scheduling. Population-average analgesic outcome expectations are not applicable.

### 6.5 Evidence Gap — Implications for Robot-Assisted TKA

Risk stratification is preoperative patient characterization and is inherently platform-independent. All five risk domains identified above apply to rTKA candidates without modification. rTKA's shorter perioperative window in same-day discharge protocols concentrates the analgesic management consequence of under-identified high-risk patients into a narrower timeframe, amplifying the clinical importance of preoperative risk screening relative to conventional TKA.

### 7.2 Principal Findings

The outcome instruments most commonly used to evaluate perioperative analgesic interventions in TKA — NRS pain scores, oral MME consumption, TUG, KOOS, WOMAC — were validated in average-risk TKA populations. The KOOS-PS MDC values (9.2–14.1 points at 80–95% confidence intervals) and PASS thresholds established by Davis et al.⁵¹ in 6,778 primary TKA patients, and the PROMIS PF MCID of approximately 7–8 T-score points established by Hung et al.⁵², are each derived from average-risk populations that do not systematically separate the high-risk phenotypes identified in Section 6.

Two instrument-level failures are documented in the mapped evidence. First, a baseline score artifact: patients with lower preoperative KOOS scores (worse function) achieve MCID at higher rates⁵⁵ — a statistical artifact of greater improvement space that does not reflect greater clinical benefit. In high-risk patients with compressed improvement space, MCID-based responder classification systematically underestimates meaningful improvement. Second, a catastrophizing moderation effect: high preoperative PCS scores significantly moderate the relationship between surgical outcome and patient-reported pain at 12 months⁵⁶, indicating that pain score change reflects different magnitudes of nociceptive change depending on baseline catastrophizing. Population-average MCID thresholds are therefore miscalibrated for high-catastrophizing patients.

The PROMIS PI domain achieved MCID in 67.2% of TKA patients versus 52.7% for PROMIS PF in a systematic review of 11,140 patients⁵³, with no significant floor or ceiling effect differences between PROMIS and legacy instruments. PROMIS PF is not uniformly more responsive than KOOS or WOMAC in general TKA populations; its potential advantage in high-risk phenotypes is mechanistically plausible but not directly validated in stratified comparison studies.

Early perioperative milestones — TUG, straight leg raise, first ambulation — are sensitive to immediate analgesic effects but demonstrate inconsistent predictive validity for 3-month and 12-month patient-reported outcomes; the predictive relationship weakens with time and is modified by preoperative psychological risk factors⁵⁴.

The Patient Global Impression of Change (PGIC) is an underutilized direct patient-anchored validity metric. PGIC functions as a patient-anchored validation metric rather than an instrument-derived proxy — enabling detection of cases where instrument failure, not treatment failure, is producing apparent non-response. Its absence as a co-primary endpoint in most TKA perioperative analgesic trials constitutes a structural gap in the outcome measurement literature.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that standard MCID thresholds for KOOS and WOMAC are likely to be poorly calibrated and not transportable without recalibration for opioid-tolerant or high-catastrophizing TKA patients.

### 7.3 Risk-Stratified Measurement Framework — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED

The following framework is EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED and PROTOCOL-CONDITIONED. It represents a hypothesis-generating structure for future prospective validation.

**Standard-risk patients:** conventional perioperative outcome battery (NRS at 6–48 hours, oral MME, TUG, KOOS-PS or WOMAC at 6 weeks/3 months/12 months) is adequate. Registry-derived MCID thresholds apply with reasonable fidelity.

**Elevated-risk patients:** supplement with PROMIS PI and PROMIS PF at 6 weeks and 3 months; include brief psychological co-measurement (PCS or PHQ-9) at postoperative assessment points. KOOS/WOMAC MCID thresholds should be interpreted with caution.

**High-risk patients:** KOOS and WOMAC alone should not constitute the primary evidence for declaring pathway success or failure. PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI, PROMIS Fatigue, and PGIC are indicated. PGIC provides the direct patient-level validity check that domain-specific instrument scores cannot: a patient reporting "much improved" on PGIC who fails to achieve KOOS-PS change thresholds represents instrument failure, not treatment failure.

### 7.4 Key Evidence Gaps

No controlled study has prospectively validated MCID recalibration or PROMIS instrument superiority over KOOS/WOMAC in specifically defined opioid-tolerant, catastrophizing, or centrally sensitized TKA subgroups. PGIC is not routinely included as a co-primary endpoint in TKA analgesic trials.

### 7.5 Evidence Gap — Implications for Robot-Assisted TKA

Outcome instruments are platform-independent as measurement tools. If rTKA produces lower baseline acute pain burden and faster early functional recovery, the score distributions in the early postoperative period may be compressed toward better outcomes relative to conventional TKA normative data — reducing detectable effect size for NRS-based endpoints and potentially revealing ceiling effects in KOOS PF subscales at shorter follow-up windows. No published study has evaluated PROM responsiveness or MCID calibration specifically in rTKA populations.

## 8. Integrated Synthesis

### 8.1 The Layered Evidence Structure

The mapped evidence across five domains indicates that perioperative analgesic planning in TKA involves a layered decision architecture in which each domain's evidence modifies the interpretation of the domains that precede it. Anesthesia modality sets the pharmacological background against which regional blocks and adjuvant agents operate. Regional analgesic technique within that background determines the anatomical distribution of analgesia and motor function preservation. Adjuvant pharmacology provides additional opioid-sparing and anti-hyperalgesic effects whose magnitude depends on the ceiling imposed by the regional and neuraxial background. Patient risk phenotype determines how much of the population-average benefit from each preceding layer any individual patient realizes. Outcome measurement determines whether that benefit — however distributed across patients — is detectable with the instruments applied.

The domain-level evidence maps interactions that are not captured in single-domain analyses. The mapped evidence for ITM opioid-sparing is attenuated in IPACK-supplemented pathways where posterior capsular coverage reduces rescue opioid demand. The mapped evidence for ketamine's analgesic contribution is concentrated in the opioid-tolerant subgroup where ceiling effects on the neuraxial-plus-block background are lowest. The MCID thresholds used to evaluate the success of both ITM and ketamine in published trials were derived from average-risk populations and are likely miscalibrated for the high-catastrophizing patients who most need effective analgesic pathway modification.

### 8.2 Evidence Quality Distribution Across Domains

The evidence base is not of uniform quality across the five mapped domains. GRADE A evidence is concentrated in the adjuvant pharmacology domain (NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors) and the regional analgesia domain (ACB over FNB for quadriceps preservation). GRADE B evidence predominates across PONV and opioid-sparing for anesthesia modality, IPACK efficacy, ketamine and dexamedetomidine as adjuvants, and risk prediction for all five preoperative risk domains. GRADE C evidence characterizes the thromboembolic and major morbidity claims for neuraxial anesthesia, IV lidocaine and magnesium as adjuvants, and the rTKA portability inferences throughout. The outcome measurement domain is methodological rather than interventional; its evidence maps instrument validity constraints rather than effect sizes.

Across all five domains, the evidence for predicting risk is stronger than the evidence for modifying outcomes based on predicted risk. This asymmetry is the most important unresolved gap in the TKA perioperative analgesic literature.

### 8.3 Cross-Domain Integration

The five mapped domains are architecturally interdependent. The anesthesia modality evidence (Section 3) shows that the neuraxial preference signal is endpoint-stratified rather than uniform; the strongest evidence rests on mechanism rather than large-trial controlled outcome data. This qualification is the correct interpretive context for the regional and adjuvant evidence that follows. The regional analgesia evidence (Section 4) shows that ACB's primary contribution in the mapped literature is motor-sparing functional preservation rather than superior pain control — a mechanistically distinct claim that shifts what outcome measures are most relevant. The adjuvant pharmacology evidence (Section 5) shows that the ceiling effect problem renders OFA bundle evidence non-portable to contemporary neuraxial-plus-block pathways; agent-specific evidence is the appropriate unit of analysis within this domain. The risk stratification evidence (Section 6) maps the patient-level modification: the effect sizes reported in Sections 3–5 were derived from populations that systematically include 20–40% of patients whose analgesic outcomes are dominated by opioid tolerance, catastrophizing, and central sensitization. The outcome measurement evidence (Section 7) maps the interpretive constraint: the instruments used to evaluate modified analgesic pathways must be capable of detecting clinically meaningful change in high-risk patients, and the mapped evidence shows that standard instruments are poorly calibrated for this purpose.

### 8.4 Evidence Gaps and Research Priorities

The rTKA evidence gap is total across all five mapped domains. No RCT or adequately designed observational study has evaluated any perioperative analgesic decision domain with rTKA as a primary platform variable. The three most clinically tractable rTKA research questions generated by the mapped evidence are: (1) does ACB's quadriceps-preservation advantage produce a measurable safety or functional benefit in rTKA ERAS protocols targeting sub-8-hour ambulation that exceeds what has been observed in conventional TKA trials with later mobilization targets? (2) does rTKA's independently lower acute pain burden produce a ceiling effect on NRS-based analgesic endpoints that reduces detectable effect sizes for regional blocks and adjuvants? (3) does the ITM monitoring requirement create a measurable barrier to same-day discharge in rTKA, and if so, does TIVA with IV dexmedetomidine provide a viable PONV risk management alternative?

Beyond rTKA, the highest-priority evidence gap is risk-stratified controlled trial evidence. The mapped literature establishes that preoperative opioid tolerance, catastrophizing, central sensitization, and psychological comorbidity predict worse analgesic outcomes; it does not establish that pathway modifications targeted to these risk factors improve outcomes in high-risk patients. Controlled trials with pre-specified composite risk stratification, risk-appropriate outcome batteries, and PGIC as a co-primary endpoint would address both the intervention evidence gap and the measurement validity gap simultaneously.

## 9. Limitations

### 9.1 Single Database Search

PubMed/MEDLINE was the sole bibliographic database searched. Embase, Cochrane Library, Scopus, and Web of Science were not searched. This increases the probability of missing relevant studies indexed in those databases. The limitation was partially mitigated by targeted guideline document retrieval and backward reference tracking from identified foundational systematic reviews; the 60 sources included in this review represent the major guideline documents, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses in each domain rather than an exhaustive study inventory. This is a transparent limitation that reduces the comprehensiveness claim of the review but does not invalidate its synthetic conclusions.

### 9.2 Single-Reviewer Screening

Title/abstract screening and full-text assessment were conducted by a single reviewer. Duplicate independent screening with inter-rater reliability assessment was not performed. This increases the risk of selection bias in source inclusion. Future work should replicate the domain-level evidence synthesis with duplicate screening to confirm the source inventory.

### 9.3 Conventional-to-rTKA Portability

All primary evidence is derived from conventional TKA or mixed arthroplasty populations. No primary evidence is specific to robot-assisted TKA. All rTKA-specific conclusions throughout this review are designated EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED. The magnitude of analgesic effect in rTKA — and in some cases the direction — cannot be established from available evidence. Prospective rTKA-specific trials are required.

### 9.4 Intervention-Layer Fragmentation and Ceiling Effects

A substantial proportion of the adjuvant pharmacology literature evaluates interventions against comparators that lack the regional block and neuraxial coverage that is standard in contemporary TKA. Effect sizes from non-block, non-neuraxial comparator trials are not directly portable to modern multimodal pathways and systematically overestimate incremental adjuvant benefit in contemporary practice.

### 9.5 Risk-Stratified Intervention Evidence

The evidence that specific pathway modifications improve outcomes in identified high-risk TKA patients is substantially weaker than the evidence that risk factors predict worse outcomes. The pathway modification framework in Section 6.4 is an evidence-gap inference, not an evidence-derived protocol, and must be evaluated prospectively before clinical implementation.

### 9.6 Outcome Measurement Validation Gaps

MCID thresholds and PROM responsiveness data are derived from average-risk TKA registry populations. No controlled study has prospectively validated MCID recalibration or instrument superiority comparisons in specifically defined high-risk TKA subgroups. The measurement framework in Section 7.3 is similarly designated EVIDENCE GAP — ANALYTICALLY INFERRED.

---

## 10. Conclusion

This scoping review mapped evidence for perioperative analgesic strategies across five clinical decision domains in adults undergoing primary TKA and characterized evidence gaps relevant to robot-assisted TKA. Sixty sources were included.

The strongest evidence supports ACB over FNB for quadriceps-sparing functional preservation and NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors as the highest-evidence adjuvant class. Moderate evidence supports neuraxial preference for PONV reduction and early opioid attenuation, IPACK as a complement to ACB for posterior capsular analgesia, ketamine for opioid-tolerant patients, and dexamethasone as a dual-function analgesic and antiemetic. Low evidence characterizes neuraxial advantage on major morbidity endpoints and IV lidocaine and magnesium as TKA adjuvants. Risk stratification evidence demonstrates strong predictive validity for preoperative opioid use and catastrophizing but weak evidence that pathway modifications improve outcomes in high-risk subgroups. Standard perioperative outcome instruments are inadequately calibrated for high-risk phenotypes, and the outcome measurement literature systematically lacks the patient-anchored validation infrastructure needed to separate instrument failure from treatment failure in stratified populations.

The rTKA evidence gap is total across all five domains. Conclusions regarding rTKA drawn in this review are evidence-gap inferences, not established findings.

Two research priorities emerge from the mapped evidence: controlled trials with pre-specified composite risk stratification and risk-appropriate outcome batteries in conventional TKA, and platform-specific controlled trials in rTKA incorporating the layered analgesic framework mapped here. Until such evidence exists, the five-domain framework developed in this review represents a structured evidence synthesis that is more comprehensive than any single-domain review and more explicitly bounded in its inferences than current clinical practice guidelines.
