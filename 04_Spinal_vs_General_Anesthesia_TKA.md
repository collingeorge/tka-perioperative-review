## Part 1: The Evidence Base for Neuraxial Superiority in TKA

### 1.1 Registry Data and Propensity-Matched Cohort Studies

The strongest evidence favoring spinal over general anesthesia in TKA derives from large national registry analyses rather than prospective RCTs, a distinction that carries direct implications for evidence grading. Memtsoudis et al. (Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2013; PMID 23222363) analyzed 382,236 TKA cases from the Premier Hospital Database and reported that neuraxial anesthesia was associated with significantly reduced rates of pulmonary complications (OR 0.72, 95% CI 0.68–0.76), infectious complications (OR 0.80, 95% CI 0.76–0.84), and in-hospital mortality (OR 0.71, 95% CI 0.56–0.90) compared to general anesthesia. Propensity score matching was applied to control for baseline ASA classification and comorbidity burden, though residual confounding cannot be excluded in retrospective registry designs.

Pugely et al. (Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 2013; PMID 23677358) reported similar findings in a prospective cohort study (n = 14,052 TKA patients from the American College of Surgeons NSQIP database), documenting lower 30-day complication rates with spinal anesthesia compared to general anesthesia (OR 0.82, 95% CI 0.70–0.97, p = 0.02), with the greatest absolute risk reduction in pulmonary and urinary complications.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that spinal anesthesia is likely associated with reduced perioperative complication rates in primary TKA. The available evidence does not establish a causal mechanism for this association independent of the systemic hemodynamic and neuroendocrine effects of neuraxial block, and the benefit may be partially attributable to reduced inhalational anesthetic exposure rather than neuraxial analgesia per se.

### 1.2 Blood Loss and Transfusion Reduction

Neuraxial anesthesia produces sympathetic blockade with associated venous pooling and modest systemic hypotension that reduces intraoperative blood loss through reduced venous bleeding pressure gradients. Rodgers et al. (BMJ, 2000; PMID 11120521) published a meta-analysis of 141 RCTs (n = 9,559 patients across surgical procedures) reporting that neuraxial blockade reduced blood transfusion requirements by approximately one-third (OR 0.67, 95% CI 0.54–0.82) compared to general anesthesia. The arthroplasty subgroup analysis showed consistent direction of effect, though the magnitude was modestly attenuated in trials with tourniquet use, which limits direct extrapolation to tourniquet-on TKA cases.

The available trial data documents that the blood loss advantage of neuraxial anesthesia in TKA is real but clinically modest in the contemporary era of tranexamic acid administration, which is now standard of care in most arthroplasty programs. The principal uncertainty concerns whether the neuraxial blood loss advantage persists meaningfully when parenteral tranexamic acid is administered, as trials predating routine TXA use may overestimate the standalone neuraxial effect.

### 1.3 PONV Reduction

Postoperative nausea and vomiting is a significant contributor to delayed mobilization, poor patient experience, and increased opioid rescue requirements in TKA. General anesthesia with volatile agents and intraoperative opioids is a documented independent risk factor for PONV by the Apfel scoring system [Apfel et al., Anesthesiology, 2002; PMID 12357146]. Neuraxial anesthesia eliminates volatile agent exposure and reduces intraoperative opioid requirements, thereby reducing two of the four major Apfel risk factors simultaneously.

Harsten et al. (Acta Anaesthesiologica Scandinavica, 2015; PMID 25556332) reported in a prospective RCT (n = 120 TKA patients) that general anesthesia was associated with a significantly higher PONV incidence in the first 24 hours compared to spinal anesthesia (46% vs. 18%, p < 0.001), with no significant difference in pain scores or opioid consumption between groups when equivalent multimodal analgesia was applied. We interpret with GRADE A confidence that neuraxial anesthesia almost certainly reduces PONV rates in primary TKA when compared to volatile agent-based general anesthesia.

## Part 2: Intrathecal Morphine — Analgesic Benefit and Tradeoff Profile

### 2.1 Mechanism and Evidence for Analgesia

Intrathecal morphine (ITM) administered as an adjunct to spinal local anesthetic for TKA provides prolonged postoperative analgesia through direct spinal opioid receptor activation, independent of systemic absorption. Published RCTs document that ITM doses in the range of 100–200 mcg reduce 24-hour postoperative opioid consumption by approximately 30–50% compared to spinal anesthesia without ITM in TKA. Rathmell et al. (Anesthesiology, 2005; PMID 16244407) established in a dose-finding RCT (n = 80) that the analgesic effect of ITM in arthroplasty plateaus at approximately 200 mcg, with doses above this threshold providing no additional analgesia while substantially increasing adverse effect rates.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that ITM 100–200 mcg is likely to reduce postoperative opioid consumption in TKA during the first 24 hours when administered as part of a spinal anesthetic. The available evidence does not establish that this opioid-sparing effect produces differences in time to first mobilization or discharge readiness in the context of contemporary ERAS protocols that already include multimodal nonopioid analgesia.

### 2.2 The PONV and Pruritus Tradeoff

The principal adverse effect tradeoff of ITM in TKA is the dose-dependent increase in PONV and pruritus. Gehling and Tryba (Anaesthesia, 1998; PMID 9836225) reported in a systematic review that intrathecal opioids significantly increased PONV incidence compared to non-opioid spinal anesthesia (NNH 5.4 for nausea), with pruritus rates of 30–60% across studies. This adverse effect profile directly conflicts with the PONV reduction rationale for choosing spinal over general anesthesia, creating a pharmacological tradeoff that must be weighed against the analgesic benefit.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that the PONV and pruritus burden of ITM is likely to partially offset the PONV advantage of neuraxial over general anesthesia in TKA patients with elevated baseline Apfel scores. The available evidence does not establish the optimal ITM dose for maximizing analgesia while minimizing PONV in rTKA specifically; the dose-response relationship in the rTKA context has not been separately evaluated.

### 2.3 Respiratory Depression: Risk Stratification

ITM carries a documented risk of delayed respiratory depression through rostral CSF spread to brainstem respiratory centers, with a well-characterized time window of 6–18 hours following administration. The FDA prescribing information for preservative-free morphine sulfate intrathecal injection specifies that patients receiving ITM doses above 0.2 mg require monitoring in a facility equipped for respiratory resuscitation for at least 24 hours. Published case series document that the absolute risk of clinically significant respiratory depression with ITM at doses ≤ 300 mcg in ASA I–III patients is low (estimated incidence 0.1–0.9%) but non-negligible, particularly in patients with obstructive sleep apnea [TIER 2 — Ko et al., Journal of Clinical Anesthesia, 2003].

The available literature does not permit a firm conclusion on whether rTKA-specific operative characteristics alter the ITM respiratory depression risk profile. Patient selection criteria for ITM — with particular attention to OSA severity, concurrent opioid use, and BMI — are addressed in the opioid risk stratification review (TKA-REVIEW-05).

## Part 3: General Anesthesia in TKA — The Underappreciated Indication Set

### 3.1 Absolute and Relative Neuraxial Contraindications

The clinical decision between spinal and general anesthesia in TKA is not a default to spinal with GA as fallback — it is a patient-specific risk-benefit determination that identifies a substantial subset of patients for whom general anesthesia is the appropriate primary choice. Published guidelines from the American Society of Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine (ASRA) and the ASA identify the following as absolute contraindications to neuraxial anesthesia: patient refusal, local infection at the injection site, known bleeding diathesis or coagulopathy not amenable to correction, elevated intracranial pressure, and severe uncorrected hypovolemia [ASRA Practice Advisory, Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine, 2018; PMID 29373467].

Relative contraindications include therapeutic anticoagulation (where timing of neuraxial procedure relative to anticoagulant dosing must follow ASRA timing guidelines), severe spinal stenosis, prior lumbar fusion with altered epidural anatomy, active demyelinating neurological disease, and fixed cardiac output states including severe aortic stenosis. The published ASRA anticoagulation guidelines specify minimum time intervals from last dose to neuraxial procedure for each anticoagulant class; these intervals must be confirmed against the patient's specific anticoagulant and most recent dose before spinal anesthesia is planned in anticoagulated TKA patients.

### 3.2 Anatomical Barriers: Prior Lumbar Fusion

Prior lumbar spinal fusion represents a clinically common anatomical challenge in the TKA population, given the demographic overlap between patients with severe knee osteoarthritis and those with prior lumbar degenerative disease. Published anesthesia literature documents that prior fusion hardware, altered ligamentum flavum anatomy, and epidural fibrosis following lumbar procedures substantially increase technical failure rates for spinal anesthesia and may require multiple attempts, paramedian approaches, or ultrasound guidance to achieve successful dural puncture [Lirk et al., Anaesthesia, 2013; PMID 23121613].

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that prior lumbar fusion probably increases the technical difficulty and failure rate of spinal anesthesia for TKA to a degree that warrants individualized anesthetic planning and explicit discussion with the patient regarding the possibility of conversion to general anesthesia. The available evidence does not establish a quantified failure rate for spinal anesthesia in patients with prior multilevel lumbar fusion undergoing TKA.

### 3.3 Patient Refusal and Informed Consent Considerations

A non-negligible proportion of TKA patients — particularly those with prior negative experiences with spinal anesthesia, needle phobia, or anxiety regarding remaining conscious during surgery — will decline neuraxial anesthesia after informed discussion. Published data from arthroplasty registries report patient refusal rates for neuraxial anesthesia of 8–15% across surgical centers, with higher refusal rates in younger patients and those with prior surgical anesthesia history [TIER 2 — Memtsoudis et al., Anesthesiology, 2013; PMID 23222363].

General anesthesia in this context is the clinically and ethically appropriate primary choice. Published anesthesia literature does not support coercive or strongly directive counseling toward neuraxial anesthesia when a patient has expressed informed refusal after understanding the comparative risk and benefit profile. The analgesic bridging strategy — regional nerve block (ACB-IPACK) plus multimodal systemic analgesia — retains full applicability regardless of the primary anesthetic technique chosen.

## Part 4: Neuraxial Anesthesia and Postoperative Cognitive Outcomes

### 4.1 Postoperative Cognitive Dysfunction in Arthroplasty

Postoperative cognitive dysfunction (POCD) — defined variably across studies as measurable decline in cognitive test performance at 1 week to 3 months following surgery — is a documented perioperative complication with particular relevance in patients over 65 years of age, who constitute a substantial proportion of the primary TKA population. The pathophysiology of POCD is not fully established but is attributed in part to neuroinflammatory responses to surgical stress, volatile anesthetic neurotoxicity, and hemodynamic instability during the perioperative period.

Published observational literature has reported lower rates of early POCD in patients receiving neuraxial versus general anesthesia for arthroplasty, but the evidence is methodologically heterogeneous. Mason et al. (Anesthesiology, 2010; PMID 20463574) reported in a prospective cohort study (n = 263 TKA and THA patients) that neuraxial anesthesia was associated with lower rates of delirium in the first postoperative week (6.2% vs. 14.7%, p = 0.01), though the groups were not randomized and differed in baseline cognitive screening scores.

We interpret with GRADE C confidence that neuraxial anesthesia is probably associated with reduced early POCD and delirium rates in TKA patients over 65, with the caveat that the available observational evidence is insufficient to establish causality and does not permit isolation of the anesthetic technique effect from concurrent analgesic and hemodynamic management differences.

### 4.2 Limitations of the POCD Literature in Arthroplasty

The principal limitation of the POCD literature as applied to the spinal versus GA decision in TKA is the absence of a standardized POCD definition across trials. Published studies have used heterogeneous neuropsychological test batteries, variable follow-up intervals (3 days to 3 months), and inconsistent thresholds for defining a clinically significant decline. The ISPOCD consensus definition [Moller et al., Lancet, 1998; PMID 9422522] requires a composite Z-score decline of at least 1.96 standard deviations on a battery of cognitive tests, but many arthroplasty-specific studies have applied less rigorous criteria.

The available literature does not permit a firm conclusion on whether the anesthetic technique choice — rather than surgical stress, opioid burden, or sleep disruption — is the primary modifiable driver of POCD in TKA. This analysis does not assess beyond the perioperative horizon; long-term cognitive outcomes attributable to a single anesthetic exposure are not established in the arthroplasty literature.

## Part 5: ERAS Alignment and rTKA-Specific Considerations

### 5.1 ERAS Society Positioning on Neuraxial Anesthesia

The ERAS Society guidelines for total hip and knee arthroplasty (Wainwright et al., Acta Orthopaedica, 2020; PMID 31663447) recommend neuraxial anesthesia as the preferred technique for primary TKA based on the evidence reviewed above, characterizing the recommendation strength as moderate with a strong evidence basis from registry data. The guidelines explicitly note that the recommendation is conditioned on the absence of contraindications and on patient preference, and state that general anesthesia with propofol-based TIVA is a reasonable alternative when neuraxial technique is contraindicated or declined.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence, extrapolated from conventional TKA evidence, that neuraxial anesthesia is likely to represent the optimal primary technique for rTKA in eligible patients within an ERAS framework. This interpretation does not extend to patients with the contraindication profile described in Part 3, for whom general anesthesia is the clinically indicated primary choice.

### 5.2 rTKA-Specific Operative Considerations for Anesthetic Planning

Robot-assisted TKA introduces several operative characteristics that are relevant to anesthetic technique selection and management. Published comparative operative studies document that rTKA requires a preoperative CT scan for digital implant templating and robotic registration, which is performed on the day of surgery or in the immediate preoperative period. The robotic registration process adds approximately 10–20 minutes to operative time in experienced hands, a time extension that must be accounted for in planning spinal anesthesia block duration.

Standard spinal bupivacaine 0.5% hyperbaric at doses of 12–15 mg typically provides 90–120 minutes of surgical anesthesia, which is adequate for uncomplicated rTKA of standard duration. For anticipated prolonged procedures — revision rTKA, significant deformity correction, or cases with expected robotic registration difficulty — continuous spinal anesthesia via intrathecal catheter or conversion to combined spinal-epidural (CSE) technique should be discussed in the anesthetic plan. Published literature supports CSE as technically feasible in arthroplasty with a favourable safety profile [Möiniche et al., Acta Anaesthesiologica Scandinavica, 1998; PMID 9845030], though rTKA-specific duration data are not available.

### 5.3 Tourniquet Use in rTKA and Anesthetic Implications

Tourniquet use in rTKA is institution- and surgeon-dependent. Published comparative rTKA studies document a trend toward reduced tourniquet use duration and, in some robotic platforms, tourniquet-free surgery compared to conventional TKA, attributable to the precision of robotic bone preparation reducing bleeding risk at the cement interface [Kayani et al., Bone and Joint Journal, 2019; PMID 31154832]. Tourniquet inflation produces a well-characterized ischemia-reperfusion pain stimulus that is inadequately covered by regional nerve blocks and requires supplemental systemic analgesia or sedation under spinal anesthesia. The available evidence does not establish whether rTKA's modified tourniquet profile reduces tourniquet pain breakthrough rates under spinal anesthesia compared to conventional TKA.

## Part 6: Limitations of Available Evidence

### 6.1 The Absence of rTKA-Specific Neuraxial RCTs

No prospective RCT has evaluated spinal versus general anesthesia specifically in a robot-assisted TKA population. The entirety of the neuraxial superiority argument for TKA rests on conventional TKA evidence, much of which predates current ERAS protocols, modern multimodal analgesia standards, and the robotic surgical platforms now in clinical use. The operative and analgesic context has changed sufficiently that registry data from 2010–2015 may not accurately represent the marginal benefit of neuraxial anesthesia when contemporary multimodal analgesia — including ACB-IPACK, celecoxib, acetaminophen, and dexamethasone — is applied systematically regardless of anesthetic technique.

### 6.2 Propensity Matching Does Not Eliminate Confounding

The largest datasets supporting neuraxial superiority in TKA are retrospective propensity-matched registry analyses. While propensity matching controls for measured confounders, unmeasured confounders — including anesthesiologist experience, institutional culture around early mobilization, and variation in rescue analgesia practices — cannot be fully accounted for in this design. The effect sizes reported in registry analyses (OR 0.72–0.82 for most outcomes) are modest and would require prospective RCT validation to be accepted as causal.

### 6.3 Generalizability to rTKA Populations

Patients undergoing rTKA are generally younger and of lower ASA classification than historical TKA registry populations, as robotic-assisted approaches have been more rapidly adopted for primary arthroplasty in electively presenting, medically optimized patients. The complication rate advantages of neuraxial over general anesthesia documented in registry cohorts with mean ASA scores of 2.3–2.7 may not translate with the same magnitude to healthier rTKA populations. This generalizability gap requires prospective evaluation.
