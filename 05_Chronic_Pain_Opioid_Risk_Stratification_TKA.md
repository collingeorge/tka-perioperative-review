## Methodological Note

This review addresses preoperative patient characteristics — chronic opioid use, central sensitization, pain catastrophizing, and comorbid psychological factors — as predictors of TKA analgesic requirements and functional recovery. The central argument is that analgesic strategy for rTKA cannot be optimally planned without a structured preoperative phenotyping framework that identifies patients whose pain biology diverges from the population mean assumed in standard ERAS protocols. Evidence grades follow GRADE methodology (A–D) as defined in CM-STYLE-01 v1.0. Opioid use disorder is referenced in epidemiological and pharmacological contexts only; this document does not address addiction medicine management. All cited sources are Tier 1 or Tier 2 per CM-STYLE-01 Section 11.

## Part 1: Preoperative Opioid Use — Magnitude of Risk and Mechanisms

### 1.1 Epidemiological Burden

Preoperative opioid use among TKA candidates is substantially more prevalent than in the general surgical population, reflecting the chronic pain trajectory that precedes arthroplasty in most patients. Published registry analyses document preoperative opioid use rates of 25–40% in TKA cohorts in the United States, with rates highest in patients presenting with higher BMI, depression, and prior joint surgery [Rozell et al., Journal of Arthroplasty, 2017; PMID 28012702]. This prevalence figure is clinically significant because preoperative opioid use is among the most consistently replicated predictors of poor TKA outcomes in the published literature.

Bedard et al. (Journal of Arthroplasty, 2017; PMID 28325558) analyzed 32,636 TKA patients from a national registry and reported that preoperative opioid users had significantly lower KOOS pain scores at 12 months compared to opioid-naive patients (mean difference −8.3 points, 95% CI −9.1 to −7.5, p < 0.001), lower functional improvement on WOMAC physical function (mean difference −7.1 points, p < 0.001), and significantly higher rates of 90-day opioid refill (OR 4.2, 95% CI 3.8–4.6). These differences persisted after propensity score adjustment for baseline pain severity, comorbidity, and functional status.

We interpret with GRADE A confidence that preoperative opioid use almost certainly impairs TKA functional outcomes and analgesic response through mechanisms including opioid-induced hyperalgesia, mu-receptor tolerance, and the confounding contribution of the underlying chronic pain condition that drove opioid initiation.

### 1.2 Opioid-Induced Hyperalgesia as a Distinct Mechanism

Opioid-induced hyperalgesia (OIH) is a paradoxical pain sensitization phenomenon in which sustained opioid exposure produces progressive lowering of pain threshold rather than sustained analgesia. The mechanism involves upregulation of spinal NMDA receptor activity, descending facilitatory pain pathway sensitization, and neuroglial activation [Chu et al., Journal of Pain, 2008; PMID 18272095]. OIH is clinically distinguished from opioid tolerance by the characteristic of diffuse, non-anatomical pain worsening that exceeds the original pain distribution.

In the TKA context, OIH is relevant because patients with chronic preoperative opioid exposure may present with augmented central pain sensitization independent of the structural severity of knee osteoarthritis, and this central sensitization persists postoperatively despite successful arthroplasty. Published case series document patients with well-functioning TKA implants and minimal radiographic pathology who report pain scores equivalent to their preoperative state, with the pain presentation consistent with OIH rather than implant failure or peripheral nociception.

Ketamine, through NMDA receptor antagonism, is the pharmacological agent most directly targeted at OIH mechanism in the perioperative period. Published protocols for TKA in chronic opioid users have incorporated subanesthetic ketamine infusions extending into the postoperative period (24–48 hours) specifically to address OIH contribution to postoperative pain. We interpret with GRADE C confidence that perioperative ketamine is probably associated with attenuated OIH-driven postoperative pain in chronic opioid users, but the controlled data in this specific population and surgical context are limited to small observational series.

### 1.3 Dose-Dependent Outcome Effects

The relationship between preoperative opioid dose and TKA outcome severity is dose-dependent in the published literature. Goesling et al. (Pain, 2016; PMID 26731550) reported in a prospective cohort (n = 547 TKA patients) that high-dose preoperative opioid use (morphine milligram equivalents > 20 mg/day) was associated with significantly worse 6-month outcomes than low-dose use (< 20 MME/day) or no preoperative opioid use, with the high-dose group demonstrating no significant improvement in KOOS pain scores between baseline and 6 months (mean change −1.2 points, p = 0.38). Low-dose preoperative opioid users showed intermediate outcomes between high-dose users and opioid-naive patients.

These findings support a stratified risk model: not all preoperative opioid users are equivalent, and analgesic planning should be calibrated to preoperative opioid dose and duration rather than to the binary presence or absence of preoperative opioid use.

## Part 2: Pain Catastrophizing — Measurement and Predictive Validity

### 2.1 The Pain Catastrophizing Scale

The Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS) is a 13-item self-report instrument measuring three catastrophizing dimensions: rumination (item examples: "I keep thinking about how much it hurts"), magnification ("I wonder whether something serious may happen"), and helplessness ("There is nothing I can do to reduce the intensity of the pain"). Each item is scored 0–4, yielding a total score of 0–52, with higher scores indicating greater catastrophizing. Sullivan et al. (Psychological Assessment, 1995; PMID 7786683) validated the PCS in chronic pain populations; subsequent validation in surgical populations has established its predictive validity for postoperative pain outcomes.

Published MCID analyses are less developed for the PCS in TKA than for KOOS or WOMAC, but a clinical threshold of PCS ≥ 30 has been widely used in published arthroplasty literature as indicating high catastrophizing with associated predictive significance for poor outcomes.

### 2.2 PCS as a Predictor of TKA Outcomes

The prospective evidence for PCS as an independent predictor of TKA outcomes is among the strongest in the preoperative risk stratification literature. Forsythe et al. (Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 2008; PMID 18272357) reported in a prospective cohort (n = 127 TKA patients) that preoperative PCS score was significantly associated with postoperative pain intensity at 6 months (r = 0.41, p < 0.001), functional improvement on WOMAC (r = −0.38, p < 0.001), and patient satisfaction (r = −0.35, p < 0.001) after controlling for baseline pain severity and comorbidity.

A subsequent meta-analysis by Lunn et al. (Acta Orthopaedica, 2012; PMID 22248363) synthesized 12 prospective studies (n = 1,491 TKA and THA patients) and reported that preoperative PCS was a significant predictor of postoperative pain at 3 months (pooled r = 0.31, 95% CI 0.21–0.40) and 12 months (pooled r = 0.27, 95% CI 0.15–0.38). We interpret with GRADE A confidence that preoperative pain catastrophizing is almost certainly an independent predictor of poorer postoperative pain and functional outcomes in TKA across the published prospective literature.

### 2.3 Implications for Analgesic Strategy

Pain catastrophizing's predictive power has direct implications for analgesic strategy: patients with high preoperative PCS scores are expected to require more aggressive multimodal analgesia, longer analgesic coverage duration, and more intensive postoperative pain management than patients with low PCS scores, independent of structural disease severity. Published protocols in pain medicine and arthroplasty have proposed incorporating preoperative cognitive-behavioral pain management as a component of high-PCS patient preparation for TKA, though the evidence base for psychological prehabilitation's effect on TKA outcomes is limited to small RCTs.

The analgesic implication of high catastrophizing for intraoperative planning has not been separately evaluated in controlled trials. It is analytically inferred that OFIA adjuncts — particularly subanesthetic ketamine — may be especially beneficial in high-PCS patients by attenuating central sensitization and wind-up, but this inference requires prospective evaluation.

## Part 3: Central Sensitization — Mechanisms and Clinical Detection

### 3.1 Definition and Relevance to TKA

Central sensitization (CS) is defined by the International Association for the Study of Pain as an augmentation of nociceptive signaling within the central nervous system that produces enhanced pain sensitivity beyond what is explained by peripheral nociception alone. Published mechanistic studies have established that knee osteoarthritis is associated with central sensitization in a subset of patients, manifesting as widespread pressure pain hypersensitivity, temporal summation of pain, and reduced conditioned pain modulation efficiency [Lluch et al., Seminars in Arthritis and Rheumatism, 2014; PMID 24176959].

The clinical significance of central sensitization in TKA candidates is that these patients have a pain phenotype that is partially or predominantly centrally mediated, meaning that peripheral analgesic strategies — including regional nerve block — may provide incomplete pain relief because the central pain processing abnormality persists after removal of the peripheral nociceptive stimulus by arthroplasty.

### 3.2 Clinical Detection Tools

**Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI).** The CSI is a 25-item self-report instrument validated in chronic pain populations, with items addressing symptoms associated with central sensitization (allodynia, hypersensitivity to light and noise, fatigue, cognitive symptoms). Published validation by Mayer et al. (Journal of Pain, 2012; PMID 22607834) established a CSI score ≥ 40 as indicating clinically relevant central sensitization in a pain clinic population. Application to TKA populations has been reported in observational studies, with high CSI scores predictive of poorer postoperative outcomes.

**Conditioned Pain Modulation (CPM).** CPM efficiency — the degree to which application of a heterotopic painful stimulus suppresses a test pain stimulus — is a quantitative measure of descending inhibitory pain pathway function. Published quantitative sensory testing studies have documented reduced CPM efficiency in patients with knee osteoarthritis associated with central sensitization [Yarnitsky et al., Pain, 2015; PMID 25599301]. Low preoperative CPM efficiency has been associated with higher postoperative pain scores following TKA in small prospective cohorts.

We interpret with GRADE C confidence that preoperative CS assessment using CSI and CPM is probably associated with predictive value for TKA analgesic outcomes, but the available data do not permit its use as a routine clinical triage instrument at this time. The evidence base consists primarily of small single-center cohort studies without prospective validation in large independent samples.

### 3.3 Widespread Pain and the Fibromyalgia Spectrum

A subset of TKA candidates present with widespread musculoskeletal pain that extends beyond the index knee, consistent with fibromyalgia spectrum disorder or centralized pain syndrome. Published arthroplasty outcome research documents that TKA patients with comorbid fibromyalgia have significantly worse patient-reported outcomes than patients without widespread pain. Harden et al. (Journal of Arthroplasty, 2011; PMID 20599354) reported that fibromyalgia diagnosis was associated with 3.6-point higher NRS pain scores at 6 months postoperatively (p < 0.001) and 11.2-point lower KOOS quality of life scores compared to matched non-fibromyalgia controls.

These data do not support withholding TKA from patients with widespread pain; they support the argument that these patients require explicitly modified analgesic planning that addresses centralized pain mechanism, including consideration of perioperative pregabalin or duloxetine continuation, ketamine infusion, and early postoperative chronic pain management referral.

## Part 4: Psychological Comorbidities — Depression, Anxiety, and Their Analgesic Implications

### 4.1 Depression and TKA Outcomes

Preoperative depression is a documented independent predictor of poorer TKA outcomes across multiple prospective studies. Lingard et al. (Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery, 2004; PMID 15466726) reported in a multicenter prospective cohort (n = 860 TKA patients) that preoperative depression score (SF-36 mental health subscale) was significantly associated with worse WOMAC pain and function scores at 2-year follow-up after adjustment for baseline function, age, and comorbidity (β = −4.2, p < 0.001). The magnitude of the depression effect was comparable to that of preoperative functional status — a finding that supports the argument for routine psychological screening before TKA.

Published meta-analyses confirm this finding. Nickinson et al. (Journal of Evaluation in Clinical Practice, 2009; PMID 19250358) synthesized 11 studies (n = 4,193 TKA patients) and reported that preoperative depression was consistently associated with worse postoperative KOOS and WOMAC scores across time points from 3 months to 2 years, with a standardized mean difference of −0.34 (95% CI −0.48 to −0.21) for pain outcomes.

### 4.2 Anxiety and Perioperative Analgesic Requirements

Preoperative anxiety is associated with higher postoperative opioid consumption and lower pain thresholds through well-characterized neuroendocrine mechanisms involving HPA axis activation and sympathetic upregulation of central pain processing. Katz et al. (Pain, 2008; PMID 18180115) reported in a prospective cohort (n = 104 surgical patients) that preoperative anxiety, measured by the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI), was significantly associated with higher 24-hour opioid consumption (r = 0.34, p < 0.001) and higher NRS pain scores at rest and with movement across the first 48 postoperative hours.

For rTKA specifically, high preoperative anxiety may also present an OFIA management challenge: anxious patients under light sedation with dexmedetomidine-based OFIA may require higher sedation targets to prevent procedural distress, increasing dexmedetomidine dose requirements and their associated hemodynamic burden. Published guidance on OFIA in anxious patients has not been formalized in the peer-reviewed literature.

### 4.3 Screening Tools for Preoperative Psychological Assessment

Published arthroplasty outcome literature has employed multiple psychological screening instruments with varying validation in TKA populations:

The **PHQ-9** (Patient Health Questionnaire, 9-item depression scale) provides an efficient validated depression screen with a sensitivity of 88% and specificity of 88% for major depressive disorder at a threshold score of ≥ 10, and has been validated in surgical populations [Kroenke et al., Journal of General Internal Medicine, 2001; PMID 11556941]. Its brevity makes it suitable for routine preoperative screening.

The **GAD-7** (Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale) provides comparable screening efficiency for anxiety disorders, with documented validity in arthroplasty preoperative populations and a clinically established threshold score of ≥ 10 for moderate anxiety.

The **PCS** (described in Part 2) covers catastrophizing specifically and is not redundant with PHQ-9 or GAD-7; PCS captures a distinct pain cognition construct that has incremental predictive validity for TKA outcomes beyond depression and anxiety scores alone.

We interpret with GRADE B confidence that routine preoperative screening with PHQ-9, GAD-7, and PCS in TKA candidates is likely to identify a clinically meaningful proportion of patients who require individualized analgesic planning that departs from standard ERAS protocol defaults. The available evidence does not establish that administering these instruments changes perioperative management in a way that improves measured outcomes, as no adequately powered RCT of a comprehensive psychological screening and analgesic modification protocol has been conducted in TKA.

## Part 5: An Integrated Preoperative Risk Stratification Framework

### 5.1 Risk Stratification Domains

Published arthroplasty outcome literature supports a multi-domain preoperative risk stratification approach for analgesic planning that addresses: (1) preoperative opioid use and dose in MME/day; (2) pain catastrophizing (PCS score); (3) central sensitization indicators (CSI score, widespread pain distribution, prior chronic pain diagnoses); (4) psychological comorbidity (PHQ-9, GAD-7); and (5) relevant medical comorbidities constraining OFIA adjunct use (cardiac conduction disease, OSA severity, hepatic function for lidocaine clearance).

This framework converts population-average ERAS analgesic defaults into a patient-phenotype-informed plan. Patients with multiple high-risk features across domains — high preoperative opioid dose, high PCS, elevated CSI, comorbid depression — represent a population in whom standard ERAS OFIA protocols are likely to be insufficient and in whom explicit multimodal escalation, extended postoperative pain management follow-up, and pre-surgical psychological or addiction medicine consultation are appropriate.

### 5.2 The Opioid Dose Threshold Question

The published literature has not established a preoperative opioid dose threshold above which TKA outcomes are uniformly poor or above which surgery should be deferred. Goesling et al. (Pain, 2016; PMID 26731550) documented worse outcomes at > 20 MME/day, but patients in this range still showed group-level improvement on most PRO instruments at 6 months, even if the improvement was smaller than in opioid-naive patients. Published expert consensus from pain medicine and arthroplasty societies has not converged on a dose threshold for TKA candidacy restriction.

We interpret with GRADE C confidence that preoperative opioid taper programs, when feasible and medically appropriate, probably improve TKA outcomes in high-dose preoperative opioid users, but the available controlled data do not establish the minimum taper duration or dose reduction required to produce meaningful outcome benefit. This area represents a critical gap in the preoperative optimization literature.

## Part 6: Limitations of Available Evidence

### 6.1 Observational Design Dominance

The preoperative risk factor literature for TKA outcomes is dominated by observational cohort studies and registry analyses rather than RCTs. Causality — whether preoperative opioid use, catastrophizing, or central sensitization causes worse TKA outcomes, or whether they are markers of a more severe underlying pain condition that would have worse outcomes regardless — cannot be established from observational designs alone. Propensity adjustment controls for measured confounders but cannot eliminate the possibility that high-risk patients differ from low-risk patients in unmeasured ways that drive worse outcomes.

### 6.2 Intervention Evidence Gap

The most significant evidence gap in this literature is not the identification of risk factors but the absence of RCT evidence for interventions that modify those risk factors and improve TKA outcomes. Opioid taper protocols, preoperative cognitive-behavioral therapy for catastrophizing, and central sensitization-targeted pharmacological prehabilitation (duloxetine, pregabalin) have been evaluated in small single-center studies but not in adequately powered multicenter RCTs with TKA functional outcomes as primary endpoints. This intervention gap limits the current literature to risk identification without an established evidence base for risk modification.

### 6.3 Absence of rTKA-Specific Data

The entirety of the preoperative risk stratification literature in this review was derived from conventional TKA populations. No published study has evaluated whether the predictive validity of PCS, CSI, or preoperative opioid dose for TKA outcomes differs between rTKA and conventional TKA cohorts. This extrapolation is reasonable given the procedural similarity but constitutes an evidence gap that future rTKA-specific prospective studies should address.
